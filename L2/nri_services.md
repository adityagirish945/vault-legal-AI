## Frequently Asked Questions

Due diligence in real estate is the process of legally verifying a property’s ownership, approvals, and compliance before purchase. It helps buyers confirm whether the property can be lawfully transferred without disputes. In practice, due diligence involves examining the title chain, sale deeds, encumbrance certificates, land use permissions, building approvals, and tax records. In Karnataka, this also includes checking Khata status, zoning compliance, and statutory clearances. The objective is to identify legal risks that may not be visible during negotiations or site visits. Proper due diligence ensures the buyer is not exposed to ownership claims, registration hurdles, or post-purchase litigation.

Legal due diligence is important because it protects buyers from ownership disputes, financial liabilities, and future legal action. It ensures the property is legally safe to purchase. Without proper verification, buyers may unknowingly acquire properties with defective titles, unpaid charges, or regulatory violations. Legal due diligence identifies such risks before money is committed. In urban markets like Bengaluru, where properties often change hands multiple times, documentation gaps can be costly. A thorough legal review helps buyers make informed decisions and prevents disputes that may arise years after purchase.

The 4 P’s of due diligence are People, Property, Process, and Paperwork. Together, they provide a structured approach to risk assessment. “People” refers to verifying the identity and authority of sellers. “Property” covers legal title, boundaries, and usage rights. “Process” involves compliance with laws, approvals, and transaction procedures. “Paperwork” includes verifying deeds, certificates, and statutory records. Addressing all four ensures the transaction is legally sound and defensible. Ignoring any one aspect can create long-term complications for the buyer.

The buyer usually pays for due diligence in a property transaction. This cost is considered part of the buyer’s risk-mitigation process. Since due diligence is conducted to protect the buyer’s interests, the expense is borne by the buyer rather than the seller. Costs may vary depending on property type, document complexity, and jurisdiction. While this may appear as an added expense, it is significantly lower than the cost of resolving disputes after purchase. Paying for due diligence upfront is widely regarded as a prudent and necessary investment.

No, bank legal verification is not enough to fully protect a property buyer. Banks focus primarily on safeguarding their loan exposure. Bank legal checks are limited to assessing whether the property can be accepted as loan security. They may not review historical ownership risks, zoning nuances, or buyer-specific concerns in detail. If the loan is rejected or closed, the buyer remains responsible for any legal issues discovered later. Independent due diligence is essential to evaluate the property from the buyer’s perspective, beyond loan eligibility.

Red flags include missing approvals, unclear title history, unpaid dues, and inconsistent records. These indicators suggest potential legal or financial risk. Common warning signs include incomplete sale deed chains, discrepancies between approved plans and actual construction, pending property taxes, or restrictive covenants. In Karnataka, absence of proper land conversion orders or Khata irregularities also signal concern. Identifying these issues early allows buyers to seek clarification, corrective action, or reconsider the transaction before committing funds.

Property documents in Karnataka are verified through sub-registrar offices, BBMP records, and revenue department databases. Each authority confirms a specific legal aspect. Title deeds and encumbrance certificates are verified at the sub-registrar office. Khata, property tax, and municipal approvals are checked with BBMP or local bodies. Land use and conversion records are reviewed through revenue authorities. Cross-verification across departments ensures document consistency and legal validity. This multi-layered approach reduces the risk of reliance on incomplete or inaccurate information.

Mandatory documents include the sale deed, encumbrance certificate, Khata, approvals, and tax receipts. These confirm legal ownership and compliance. Additional documents such as occupancy and completion certificates, building plan approvals, and land conversion orders may apply depending on property type. These records establish that the property was legally developed and is fit for occupation. Absence of any critical document can delay registration or create future complications. Buyers should ensure all mandatory documents are verified before proceeding.

Due diligence typically takes between 7 and 21 days, depending on the property type and document availability. More complex properties require additional time. Apartments with clear records may be reviewed faster, while land or independent houses often involve deeper verification. Delays may occur if records are outdated or scattered across departments. Rushing this process increases risk. A reasonable due diligence period allows thorough review and informed decision-making.

By reviewing title history, encumbrance records, and prior transactions, due diligence identifies whether a property has been pledged or sold previously. It also checks for inconsistencies between seller claims and official records. While no process can eliminate risk entirely, proper legal verification provides strong protection and helps buyers avoid entering disputed transactions.
