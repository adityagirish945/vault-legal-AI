1. LEGAL DEFINITION, SCOPE AND IMPORTANCE IN KARNATAKA

1.1 Definition and objectives

* In Karnataka practice, “property due diligence” is the legal and factual investigation of a property’s title, encumbrances, approvals, statutory compliances and possession before concluding a transfer or creation of rights (sale, mortgage, lease, development, etc.).legalkart+1

* Typical Bengaluru-focused guides describe it as verifying ownership documents, encumbrance certificates, approvals from BBMP/BDA and other authorities, Khata, tax receipts and litigation status so that the buyer obtains a clear, marketable and mortgage‑able title.vaultproptech+2

1.2 Legal foundation

* Under the Transfer of Property Act, a valid sale of immovable property requires a competent transferor, a transferee capable of taking, and transfer of ownership for a price through a registered instrument when value exceeds ₹100, making verification of title and registration a legal necessity.adityabirlacapital+1

* Section 54 of the Transfer of Property Act mandates that sale of tangible immovable property of value ₹100 or more can be made only by a registered instrument, which ties due diligence directly to the Registration Act process at the Sub‑Registrar’s office.drishtijudiciary+1

* Section 55 of the Transfer of Property Act imposes duties on the seller to disclose material defects and to produce all title deeds for buyer’s inspection, which practically operates through the advocate’s due diligence exercise.\[[adityabirlacapital](https://www.adityabirlacapital.com/abc-of-money/transfer-of-property-act-india)\]​

1.3 Regulatory overlay in Karnataka

* Real Estate (Regulation and Development) Act, 2016 (RERA) as implemented in Karnataka requires project registration, disclosure of title and approvals, and imposes obligations on promoters to transfer clear title by conveyance deed, making independent verification by buyers essential.rera.karnataka+3

* Land use and conversion are governed by the Karnataka Land Revenue Act, under which Section 95 requires DC conversion for using agricultural land for non‑agricultural (residential/commercial) purposes, so due diligence must verify land classification and conversion orders.vaultproptech+2

1.4 Why it is critical in Bengaluru/Karnataka

* Bengaluru has a documented problem of “revenue sites” (agricultural/revenue land sold for housing without DC conversion or BDA/BBMP approvals), unauthorized layouts and buildings, which carry high risks of demolition, denial of utilities and inability to obtain loans or Khata.commonfloor+3

* Courts and BBMP have tightened enforcement against unauthorized constructions and OC violations, including Supreme Court‑mandated demolition of illegal buildings and denial of utilities to properties without valid Completion/Occupancy Certificates, making prior legal scrutiny indispensable.sairamlawassociates+2

2. WHEN DUE DILIGENCE IS REQUIRED (TYPICAL SCENARIOS)

2.1 Resale (secondary) apartment / villa / independent house

* For resale units, buyers must verify at least 30‑year title chain via prior sale deeds or mother deed, Encumbrance Certificates (EC), Khata, tax receipts, approved plan, OC/CC (if applicable), and any association NOCs, as emphasized in Bengaluru‑specific resale checklists.ecospacerealtors+3

* Resale properties often carry past mortgages, family settlements or unregistered inheritances; EC, court‑case searches and revenue records are needed to detect mortgages, court attachments or lis pendens affecting the title.onecityproperty+2

2.2 New apartment from builder / under‑construction project

* For primary sales from builders, guides for Bengaluru stress verifying project RERA registration, title of project land, joint development agreements (if any), sanctioned building plan, commencement certificate, OC/CC status, and all NOCs (BWSSB, BESCOM, pollution control, airport, etc.).proplex.co+2

* Karnataka RERA requires eligible projects to be registered on the KRERA portal with disclosures of approvals, land title and timeline, so buyers are expected to cross‑check builder claims against the online KRERA record and prior orders.bajajfinserv+3

2.3 Agricultural land and DC conversion / plotted development

* For agricultural land or layouts on erstwhile agricultural land, land use conversion (DC conversion) under Section 95 of the Karnataka Land Revenue Act is mandatory before it can be legally used for residential/commercial purposes, and authorities may levy penalties or demolish structures if conversion is not obtained.housing+2

* Due diligence must therefore cover RTC/Pahani, mutation entries, conversion order, revenue maps (via Bhoomi portal), and ensure the plot is not a “revenue site” lacking BDA/BBMP/BMRDA approval or lying in green‑belt/regulated zones.blog.maadiveedu+4

2.4 NRI‑owned property

* For assets owned by NRIs, practitioners recommend enhanced verification of authority and consent—checking POA validity, FEMA compliance where relevant, and ensuring the person executing the deed has properly executed and registered power of attorney, alongside the standard Karnataka title and approval checks.legalkart+1

* NRIs can apply online for BBMP e‑Khata and use portals such as Kaveri Online Services and KRERA from abroad, but may need to authorize local representatives for physical appearances and certain submissions.karnataka+2

2.5 Inherited / partitioned property

* Where title arises from inheritance or family partition, due diligence must review succession documents (probate/Will, legal heirship, family settlement, partition deeds) and confirm that all co‑heirs have properly conveyed their shares, as advised in Indian litigation‑check guides.thelegalwatch+1

* EC, RTC/mutation, and Khata should reflect the latest owner or all co‑owners; any mismatch between revenue/municipal records and title documents is treated as a red flag that requires rectification before purchase.bricknbolt+2

2.6 Auctioned property (bank, DRT, SARFAESI, court auction)

* In auction purchases, the buyer typically gets a sale certificate rather than a standard sale deed, but must still verify the underlying title chain, encumbrances, and ensure that all prior charges have been cleared or disclosed, in line with general litigation‑risk guidance.legitquest+1

* Courts and authorities treat buyers as bound by the auction conditions; failure to conduct independent due diligence can leave the purchaser exposed to prior defects, claims by third parties, or disputes over possession despite having a sale certificate.bajajfinserv+1

3. GOVERNING BODIES AND KEY DATA SOURCES

3.1 IGR Karnataka – Sub‑Registrar Offices & Kaveri Online Services

* The Department of Stamps and Registration (IGR Karnataka) maintains registered documents (sale deeds, mortgages, leases, powers of attorney, etc.) and issues Encumbrance Certificates and certified copies through Sub‑Registrar Offices and the Kaveri Online Services portal.igr.karnataka+1

* Kaveri Online Services allows citizens to search property valuation, apply for and download EC and certified copies, calculate stamp duty/registration fees, and book registration slots, making it the primary electronic source for title‑related records in Karnataka.cleartax+4

3.2 BBMP / urban local bodies (Khata, tax, OC/CC, building approvals)

* Bruhat Bengaluru Mahanagara Palike (BBMP) is the municipal corporation for Bengaluru and maintains property tax records, issues Khata (including e‑Khata), approves building plans within its jurisdiction, and issues Completion Certificates (CC) and Occupancy Certificates (OC) for compliant buildings.architects4design+3

* BBMP’s e‑Aasthi and e‑Khata systems record ownership, built‑up area and tax status; BBMP property tax portals provide online tax payment history and receipts, which are primary due‑diligence evidence of municipal recognition and compliance.docupro+4

3.3 Bangalore Development Authority (BDA) and other planning authorities

* BDA is the principal planning authority for core Bengaluru, responsible for comprehensive development plans, layout approvals, site allotment and ensuring that layouts comply with roads, parks and civic amenity norms.realtylegal+2

* BDA approval of a layout is often a pre‑condition for obtaining A‑Khata and BBMP building plan sanctions in its jurisdiction, so buyers must verify BDA approval certificates and cross‑check sites on the BDA portal or in person.vaultproptech+2

3.4 Karnataka RERA (KRERA)

* The Karnataka Real Estate Regulatory Authority portal provides project and agent registration details, including approvals, project timelines, litigation orders, and revenue recovery certificate lists for non‑compliant promoters.leasewarehouse+3

* Buyers can use KRERA “Project Status” and cause‑list services to verify whether a project is registered, whether complaints or revenue recovery proceedings exist, and to download certified copies of RERA orders for deeper due diligence.services.india.gov+2

3.5 Revenue Department & Bhoomi land records

* Karnataka’s Bhoomi system provides digitized land records such as RTC (Record of Rights, Tenancy and Crops), mutation extracts, Khata extract and revenue maps, making it the key source for verifying agricultural and converted‑land titles.rtcbhoomionline+1

* Bhoomi and related land‑conversion modules allow checking of dispute status, pending conversion requests and final DC conversion orders, which are critical for assessing whether non‑agricultural use (layouts/sites) is legally permitted.architects4design+3

3.6 BWSSB and utility agencies

* Bengaluru Water Supply and Sewerage Board (BWSSB) processes water and sewerage connections based on sanctioned building plans, tax receipts and, for multi‑dwelling units, Occupancy Certificates, and thus indirectly confirms whether a building has complied with planning norms.architects4design+1

* BWSSB requires sanctioned plans or tax receipts from BBMP/BDA and other local authorities, and delays or denials of connections where OC is missing serve as practical indicators of unauthorized construction risk.ujjivansfb.bank+2

3.7 Judiciary and litigation portals

* High Court of Karnataka and district courts publish case status online through state and e‑Courts portals, where cases can be searched by party name, case number, advocate name, FIR number, Act and section.services.ecourts.gov+4

* National portals like the National Judicial Data Grid and judgment search portals allow advocates to search for pending and disposed litigation involving parties, survey numbers or project names, which is now standard in litigation‑focused property due diligence.doj.gov+2

4. COMPLETE DUE DILIGENCE CHECKLIST

(Indicative, to be tailored to property type and facts; all items are Karnataka/Bengaluru‑specific practice.)

4.1 Title chain (minimum 30 years)

* Collect and review all title documents forming an unbroken chain for at least 30 years: prior sale deeds, gift deeds, partition/family settlement deeds, release deeds, grant or allotment orders, rectification deeds and Joint Development Agreements where applicable.brigadegroup+3

* Verify that each transferor had the right to convey (ownership, competency under Transfer of Property Act) and that property descriptions, boundaries and measurements remain consistent across the chain.drishtijudiciary+2

4.2 Encumbrance Certificate (EC) – 30 years or from root of title

* Obtain EC in Form 15/16 from the Sub‑Registrar via Kaveri Online or offline, typically for at least 30 years or from the date of first conveyance, to capture all registered transactions (sales, mortgages, leases, attachments, etc.).housing+2

* Check that all known deeds in the title chain appear correctly; investigate any mortgages, court‑attachment entries, agreements of sale, or discrepancies between EC and documents, and confirm that prior charges have been cleared.onecityproperty+2

4.3 Khata certificate and Khata extract / e‑Khata

* For BBMP/GBA areas, obtain Khata certificate and Khata extract or e‑Khata, which record property details (PID, extent, usage), owner name and tax assessment, and are required for services like building plan sanction and utilities.vaultproptech+2

* Verify that the Khata is in the seller’s name (or builder’s association where appropriate), confirms correct classification (A‑Khata vs revenue/panchayat Khata), and matches survey numbers, dimensions and usage stated in title deeds.mypatta+2

4.4 Property tax receipts

* Collect latest BBMP property tax receipts (typically for at least 3–5 years) through the BBMP tax portal or physical records to confirm timely payment and municipal recognition of the property.ujjivansfb+2

* Cross‑verify that the SAS/PID or Khata number, owner name, built‑up area and usage in tax records align with Khata and title documents, as mismatches may indicate unauthorized extensions or incorrect classification.hdfcergo+2

4.5 Approved layout plan / building plan and sanctions

* For plots and buildings, obtain sanctioned layout plan and building plan from BDA/BBMP/BMRDA or the competent authority, ensuring that plot number, dimensions, FAR, setbacks and number of floors approved match the constructed reality.bricknbolt+2

* Check that the project falls under the correct planning body (BDA vs BMRDA vs MPA vs Panchayat) and that approvals are genuine and duly issued, since construction without or beyond sanctioned plans can attract demolition or penalties.fourquarters+2

4.6 Commencement Certificate (CC) and Occupancy Certificate (OC)

* For multi‑dwelling and larger projects, obtain Commencement Certificate (permission to start construction) and Occupancy Certificate from BBMP or other local bodies, as these certify that construction adheres to sanctioned plans and is fit for occupation.bajajfinserv+2

* Absence of OC/CC is a critical red flag because courts and municipal circulars have linked OC to legality of occupation, eligibility for utilities and loan sanction, and unauthorized buildings have been ordered to be demolished or denied services.nobrokerage+3

4.7 RERA registration and compliance

* For eligible real estate projects (broadly above specified area/units thresholds), verify project registration on the Karnataka RERA portal, including registration number, promoter details, land title, approvals and committed completion date.rera.karnataka+2

* Review any KRERA orders, complaints, revenue recovery certificates or cause‑list entries for the project or promoter, as these reveal regulatory non‑compliance, delays or refund/penalty proceedings.godrejcapital+2

4.8 Revenue records – RTC / Pahani, mutation, land conversion and maps

8. KEY LEGAL PROVISIONS RELEVANT TO CLEAR TITLE

8.1 Registration Act, 1908 (as applicable in Karnataka)

* The Registration Act consolidates rules for registration of documents and makes registration compulsory for instruments of gift of immovable property and for non‑testamentary instruments creating or assigning any right, title or interest in immovable property of value ₹100 and upwards.\[[legitquest](https://www.legitquest.com/act/registration-act-1908/6297)\]​

* Without proper registration, an instrument required to be registered cannot affect immovable property, confer power to adopt, or be received as evidence of a transaction affecting such property (subject to limited exceptions), which is why checking registration of all title documents is central to due diligence.\[[legitquest](https://www.legitquest.com/act/registration-act-1908/6297)\]​

8.2 Transfer of Property Act, 1882

* Section 7 specifies who is competent to transfer (person competent to contract and entitled to transferable property), requiring verification that each transferor in the title chain had legal capacity and title.\[[adityabirlacapital](https://www.adityabirlacapital.com/abc-of-money/transfer-of-property-act-india)\]​

* Section 8 provides that a transfer passes all the interest which the transferor is capable of passing, underscoring the need to confirm that no encumbrances or limited rights restrict what is being transferred.\[[adityabirlacapital](https://www.adityabirlacapital.com/abc-of-money/transfer-of-property-act-india)\]​

* Section 54 defines “sale” of immovable property as transfer of ownership for a price and requires that sales of tangible immovable property of value above ₹100 be made only by registered instrument, tying title validity to registration records.\[[drishtijudiciary](https://www.drishtijudiciary.com/to-the-point/ttp-transfer-of-property-act/sale-of-immovable-property)\]​

* Section 55 lists seller’s duties, including to disclose material defects, produce title deeds for buyer’s inspection and answer all relevant questions, which forms the statutory backdrop for title‑verification and advocate’s queries during due diligence.\[[adityabirlacapital](https://www.adityabirlacapital.com/abc-of-money/transfer-of-property-act-india)\]​

8.3 Real Estate (Regulation and Development) Act, 2016 – as implemented in Karnataka

* Karnataka RERA portal and guidance emphasize that all eligible projects must be registered, with promoters required to disclose sanctioned plans, layout plans, land title and encumbrances, and to keep information updated.godrejcapital+2

* Section 17 of RERA requires the promoter to execute a registered conveyance deed and transfer undivided proportionate title in common areas, and to hand over possession and title documents to allottees or associations, reinforcing the importance of verifying whether and how conveyance and hand‑over obligations are met.registerkaro+1

8.4 Karnataka Land Revenue Act, 1964 – Section 95 (DC conversion)

* DC‑conversion guides explain that under Section 95 of the Karnataka Land Revenue Act, agricultural land cannot legally be used for non‑agricultural purposes (residential, commercial, industrial) without prior conversion order by the Deputy Commissioner.vaultproptech+2

* Failure to obtain conversion exposes the owner or purchaser to legal action, penalties and possible removal of structures, so due diligence must ensure that DC conversion orders exist, cover the property, and their conditions are complied with.housing+1

8.5 Municipal and OC‑related jurisprudence

* BBMP‑focused legal analyses highlight that municipal bodies are bound to refuse utilities and trade licenses and to act against unauthorized constructions lacking valid OC/CC, following Supreme Court and High Court directions.sairamlawassociates+1

* Practical legal advice to Bengaluru buyers emphasizes that purchasing flats without OC can lead to demolition risk, disconnection of basic services, penalties, loan denials and poor resale value, aligning due‑diligence importance with these judicial and municipal mandates.lawrato+2

9. MAJOR RED FLAGS THAT SHOULD TRIGGER CAUTION OR HALT A TRANSACTION

9.1 Pending or serious litigation

* Litigation‑check guides warn that properties involved in pending suits for partition, specific performance, injunction, or attachment can result in years of litigation, financial loss and even eviction for buyers who purchase without full knowledge.thelegalwatch+1

* Discovery of suits, decrees, or attachments relating to the property, sellers or co‑owners in High Court or district‑court records—especially where not disclosed in EC—is a strong reason to pause the transaction until risks are fully assessed and mitigated.karnatakajudiciary.kar.nic+2

9.2 Revenue‑site classification and lack of DC conversion

* Revenue‑site analyses for Bangalore stress that sites formed on agricultural or village land without DC conversion and BDA/BBMP/BMRDA approvals are inherently high‑risk, often lacking proper civic amenities and facing potential acquisition or demolition without compensation.nobroker+2\[[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YbJ0SQ1-uHo)\]​

* Experts caution that non‑converted agricultural land used for residential purposes in Karnataka violates Section 95 of the Land Revenue Act and is liable for penalties and removal of structures, making absence of DC conversion order a major red flag.vaultproptech+2

9.3 Missing Occupancy Certificate (OC) / serious plan deviations

* Legal‑advice portals and banking guides consistently describe purchase of flats without OC as risky because unauthorized buildings can be demolished, utilities withdrawn, and eviction notices issued, and banks may refuse to sanction loans on such properties.bajajfinserv+3

* Recent Supreme Court‑linked guidance in Bengaluru directs BBMP and utilities not to provide or to withdraw water, sewerage and electricity from unauthorized constructions without OC/CC, and to take action against erring officials, making lack of OC a transaction‑stopping issue in many cases.nobrokerage+1

9.4 Encumbrances, mortgages and adverse EC entries

* EC entries showing subsisting mortgages, charges, court attachments, or prior sale/agreements inconsistent with the seller’s claim signal that clear and marketable title does not yet exist and that discharge documents or NOCs are required before proceeding.cleartax+1

* Guides on checking land title in Bangalore recommend treating non‑“Nil” EC entries relating to loans, lis pendens or undisclosed agreements as red flags until independently verified and resolved through proper releases and fresh EC.vaultproptech+1

9.5 Forged, inconsistent or incomplete documentation

* Property‑verification services list common red flags such as discrepancies between survey numbers or boundaries across title, Khata and RTC; missing links in the title chain; unregistered family settlements; and forged or altered documents.legalkart+1

* Where seller’s name is not reflected uniformly in EC, Khata, RTC and tax records, or key documents (e.g., JDA, GPA) are not registered or appear suspicious, good practice is to halt or at least defer the transaction pending rectification and expert opinion.bhoomirtconline.co+2

9.6 Lack of proper approvals and RERA non‑compliance

* Buying in non‑approved layouts (no BDA/BMRDA/MPA approval) or buildings without sanctioned plans or with significant deviations beyond permissible limits exposes buyers to demolition, penalties and denial of OC, and is flagged as a major risk by Bengaluru real‑estate and legal guides.fourquarters+2

* Absence of mandatory RERA registration for eligible projects, or presence of adverse orders and revenue‑recovery certificates against the promoter on KRERA, are strong indicators to avoid or reassess a project.bajajfinserv+2

10. CONSEQUENCES OF SKIPPING OR DILUTING DUE DILIGENCE (PRACTICAL RISKS)

10.1 Disputed titles and endless litigation

* Indian property‑litigation guidance warns that buying without full title and court‑case verification can leave purchasers embroiled in long‑running disputes, including challenges from legal heirs, prior purchasers or creditors, leading to attachment, injunctions and potential loss of possession.housivity+1

* Where partition or specific‑performance suits exist, courts may set aside or ignore later transfers, meaning that buyers who fail to detect such cases risk having their sale deeds declared ineffective or being drawn into complex multi‑party litigation.karnatakajudiciary.kar.nic+1

10.2 Illegal constructions, demolition and utility disconnection

* Legal opinions about flats without OC in Bangalore emphasize that such buildings can be declared unauthorized and ordered demolished, with residents required to vacate and facing loss of investment despite having sale deeds.lawrato+2

* Supreme Court‑compliant BBMP guidelines now contemplate denying or withdrawing electricity, water and sewerage connections to buildings lacking valid OC/CC, placing occupants in precarious positions where even basic habitability cannot be assured.ujjivansfb.bank+2

10.3 Loan rejection and financing difficulties

* Banks and financial institutions frequently insist on clear EC, Khata and OC before sanctioning home or loan‑against‑property facilities, and guides to OC in Bangalore explicitly state that absence of OC can lead to rejection of loan applications.bajajfinserv+2

* Where titles are unclear (revenue sites without DC conversion, missing approvals, or litigation), lenders either refuse financing or offer it on unfavourable terms, restricting the buyer’s ability to leverage the asset or resell to financed purchasers.nobroker+2

10.4 Revenue‑site and non‑converted land risks – loss of investment

* Analyses of revenue sites in Karnataka highlight that owners may be unable to legally construct, obtain Khata, secure utilities or resell at full value, and that government regularization policies are uncertain and subject to change, leaving buyers exposed to long‑term legal limbo.commonfloor+3

* DC‑conversion guides caution that failure to obtain conversion exposes properties to penalties and potential removal of structures, implying that buyers of non‑converted agricultural plots can face near‑total loss if authorities enforce land‑use norms strictly.aakruthiproperties+3

10.5 Regulatory enforcement and RERA actions

* KRERA has powers to impose penalties, order refunds with interest and initiate revenue‑recovery proceedings against non‑compliant promoters, and its revenue‑recovery certificate lists can signal serious financial and compliance trouble in a project.hindustantimes+2

* Buyers who ignore RERA status and orders may be tied to delayed or stalled projects, subject to litigation before RERA and appellate authorities, and dependent on enforcement of orders that might take years, instead of pre‑screening risk through RERA‑based due diligence.leasewarehouse+1

10.6 Depressed resale value and marketability

* Bengaluru‑specific property checklists warn that flats or plots without key documents (OC, RERA registration, A‑Khata, DC conversion, sanctioned plan) suffer from reduced resale demand and lower valuations, as prudent buyers and banks shy away from such properties.vaultproptech+2

* Law‑practice commentary notes that absence of OC/CC or existence of serious deviations may make properties practically unsaleable in the formal market, as subsequent purchasers demand heavy discounts or insist on full regularization before agreeing to transact.lawrato+2

10.7 Overall risk of total or substantial loss

* Combined guidance from legal, planning and revenue sources in Karnataka underscores that skipping due diligence can result in buyers paying for assets that cannot be lawfully used, financed or sold, and that may be demolished or acquired without adequate compensation.commonfloor+3

* In contrast, investing modest time and cost in systematic due diligence—covering title chain, EC, Khata, tax, approvals, RERA, revenue and litigation—substantially mitigates risk and aligns with statutory duties and market practice in Bengaluru property transactions.nobroker+3
