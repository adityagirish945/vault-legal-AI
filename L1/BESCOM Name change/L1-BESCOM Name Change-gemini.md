# **Comprehensive Authoritative Reference on BESCOM Installation Transfer and Name Change Protocols in Bangalore Jurisdiction**

The administrative and legal framework governing the transfer of electricity connections within the Bangalore Electricity Supply Company Limited (BESCOM) jurisdiction represents a critical intersection of utility regulation and property law. In the state of Karnataka, the electricity connection is not merely a utility service but a contractual agreement between a distribution licensee and a consumer, governed by the overarching principles of the Indian Electricity Act, 2003, and the specific regulations promulgated by the Karnataka Electricity Regulatory Commission (KERC).1 For legal professionals, property consultants, and homeowners, understanding the nuances of the "Transfer of Installation"—the formal term for a name change—is essential for ensuring clear title, regulatory compliance, and access to state-mandated welfare schemes.4

## **Legal Conceptualization and Statutory Importance of Installation Transfer**

The transfer of an electricity installation is defined as the formal novation of the Power Supply Agreement (PSA) between the utility provider and the registered consumer. Legally, the electricity bill does not serve as a primary title document to a property; however, it is considered a vital piece of secondary evidence demonstrating possessory interest and occupancy.5 Under the Karnataka Electricity Regulatory Commission (Electricity Supply) Code, 2004, a "Consumer" is defined as any person who is supplied with electricity for their own use by a licensee, the Government, or any other person engaged in the business of supplying electricity to the public under the Act.1

### **The Contractual Nexus and Novation**

The relationship between BESCOM and its customers is governed by a contract. When a property is sold, gifted, or inherited, this contractual nexus must be formally updated to reflect the new owner's identity. This process involves the termination of the existing agreement and the execution of a fresh Power Supply Agreement.7 The "Installation" comprises the meter, service line, and associated apparatus used for the supply of electricity to a specific Revenue Registration (RR) Number.9 The legal importance of this change is multifold:

| Legal Dimension | Significance and Practical Implications |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Contractual Liability** | The registered consumer is legally responsible for all dues, arrears, and penalties. Failure to transfer the connection means the previous owner remains liable for future consumption or illegal activities (like meter tampering) performed by the current occupant.7 |
| **Proof of Occupancy** | Utility bills are accepted by various government and private bodies as valid proof of address for the issuance of passports, voter IDs, and driving licenses.6 |
| **Due Diligence in Real Estate** | In property transactions, banks and financial institutions conduct due diligence by verifying utility records. Discrepancies between the property title and utility bills can delay or disqualify home loan applications.5 |
| **Statutory Recoveries** | Under the Karnataka Power Transmission Corporation (Recovery of Dues) Act, 1976, BESCOM has the power to recover arrears as if they were arrears of land revenue. These proceedings are initiated against the person listed in the utility's records.12 |

### **Administrative Significance: The Gruha Jyothi Factor**

A contemporary development adding significant weight to the requirement of name change is the Karnataka Government’s Gruha Jyothi Scheme. This scheme provides up to 200 units of free electricity per month to eligible domestic households.4 Eligibility is strictly linked to the Aadhaar of the resident and the RR Number. If the utility record remains in the name of a previous owner or a deceased individual, the current resident is unable to successfully link their Aadhaar for subsidy benefits, leading to immediate financial loss.4

## **Institutional Oversight and Regulatory Framework**

The management of electricity distribution in Bangalore is under the exclusive jurisdiction of the Bangalore Electricity Supply Company Limited (BESCOM). The institutional structure is designed to handle both urban metropolitan areas and rural districts.

### **Jurisdictional Divisions**

BESCOM's administrative hierarchy is organized into Zones, Circles, Divisions, and Sub-divisions. For the purpose of installation transfer, the most relevant entity is the jurisdictional Sub-division Office (SDO) and its corresponding Operations and Maintenance (O\&M) Unit.9

* **Sub-division Office (SDO)**: Managed by an Assistant Executive Engineer (AEE), this office handles the administrative approval of name changes, fee collection at the cash counter, and final record updates in the R-APDRP system.7  
* **Operations and Maintenance (O\&M) Unit**: Managed by a Junior Engineer (JE) or Assistant Engineer (AE), this unit is responsible for the technical verification of the meter, assessing the actual consumption at the site, and certifying the application file.9

### **Primary Regulatory Instruments**

The process is governed by several key statutes and regulations:

1. **The Electricity Act, 2003**: The primary national legislation governing the generation, transmission, and distribution of electricity.1  
2. **KERC (Electricity Supply) Code, 2004**: Provides the specific rules for the "Transfer of Installation" under Clause 36.00 (and previously referenced under Chapter IX in older versions).2  
3. **Karnataka Electricity Distribution Code (KEDC) 2025**: The newly notified code aimed at modernizing grid connectivity and prosumer standards, which is set to supersede the 2015 version.18  
4. **Karnataka Sakala Services Act, 2011**: Mandates time-bound delivery of government services, including electricity connection transfers.9

## **Scenarios Requiring Installation Transfer**

The requirement for an installation transfer is triggered by specific legal events that alter the ownership or occupancy status of a premise. Each scenario demands a different set of evidentiary documents to prove the legitimate transition of interest.

### **Property Sale and Purchase**

This is the most common scenario. Upon the registration of a Sale Deed, the buyer acquires the legal title to the property. However, the electricity connection remains a separate contract that must be transferred from the "Vendor" (seller) to the "Vendee" (buyer). In large apartment complexes, this often involves a two-step process: first from the builder to the first owner, and subsequently from owner to owner in resale cases.4

### **Death of the Registered Consumer**

When the person whose name appears on the electricity bill passes away, the account becomes legally "orphaned." It is mandatory for the legal heirs to transfer the connection to their name. Failure to do so can lead to complications in future property successions and may result in the utility company disconnecting the supply if the contract is deemed void due to the death of the signatory.6

### **Inheritance and Partition**

In cases of ancestral property division, a Partition Deed is executed. Each beneficiary of a specific portion of the property must transfer the corresponding electricity installation to their individual name to ensure separate liability for consumption and dues.7

### **Gift Deeds and Settlement Deeds**

A Gift Deed involves the transfer of property without monetary consideration. The donee (receiver of the gift) must follow the same procedure as a purchaser, presenting the registered Gift Deed as proof of title to BESCOM.13

### **Long-term Tenancy**

While the property title remains with the landlord, certain long-term lease agreements (especially commercial) require the utility bill to be in the tenant's name for tax compliance or business licensing. KERC regulations allow for "Proof of Occupancy" to be used for name changes in specific categories, provided an Indemnity Bond and Owner’s NOC are submitted.7

## **Scenario-Specific Step-by-Step Procedures**

The transfer process in Bangalore can be initiated through two primary routes: the Online Fast Track route and the traditional Offline route. While the online route is publicized, the offline route is often considered more efficient for complex scenarios like inheritance due to the necessity of presenting original documents for verification.9

### **Scenario A: Transfer After Property Purchase (Standard Sale)**

This procedure applies to both residential apartments and independent houses in Bangalore.

1. **Arrears Verification**: The purchaser must ensure the latest bill is paid. Any outstanding interest or "short-claim" dues must be cleared before the application is accepted.7  
2. **Document Notarization**: Obtain notarized copies of the Registered Sale Deed. If the original is with a bank for a home loan, a certified copy from the bank or a notarized copy of the photocopy is required.6  
3. **Stamp Paper Preparation**: Purchase two E-Stamp papers of ₹500 each. One is utilized for the **Power Supply Agreement (Annexure-7)** and the other for the **Indemnity Bond (Annexure-9)**.9  
4. **NOC Procurement**: Obtain the signature of the seller on the Consent Letter (Form IV of the transfer application). This letter authorizes the transfer of the installation and the security deposits held by BESCOM.9  
5. **Technical Verification (O\&M Visit)**: Visit the local O\&M unit. Provide a clear photograph of the meter showing the current reading and serial number. The Junior Engineer (JE) verifies this against the records, signs the "Office Use" section of the application, and affixes the unit's seal.9  
6. **Administrative Submission (SDO Visit)**: Submit the verified file at the Sub-division Office. The cashier collects the transfer fee (₹100 for LT). Ensure you receive a payment receipt and, more importantly, a **GSC Number** for Sakala tracking.4  
7. **Final Update**: The file is processed internally. The name update typically reflects in the next billing cycle, which can be verified on the BESCOM Mithra app.9

### **Scenario B: Transfer After Death of Registered Consumer**

This process is more legally intensive as it requires proving the right of succession.

1. **Succession Documentation**: Obtain the Death Certificate and the Succession Certificate or a Legal Heirship Certificate from the Tahsildar or a Civil Court.9  
2. **Affidavits of Consent**: If there are multiple legal heirs (e.g., three siblings), and the connection is to be transferred to only one, the other heirs must provide a "No Objection Affidavit" on stamp paper.26  
3. **Indemnity Execution**: The applicant must execute an Indemnity Bond (Annexure-9) stating they will indemnify BESCOM against any future legal disputes arising from other claimants to the deceased person's estate.7  
4. **Fresh Agreement**: The legal heir executes a fresh Power Supply Agreement in their name.7  
5. **SDO Verification**: The Assistant Executive Engineer (AEE) reviews the succession documents. Since the "Existing Consumer" cannot sign an NOC, the transfer is approved based on the Indemnity Bond and the Succession Certificate.8

### **Scenario C: Transfer via Inheritance, Gift, or Partition**

1. **Title Deed Submission**: Present the Registered Partition Deed or Gift Deed. In partition cases, the specific schedule of the property mentioned in the deed must correspond to the address of the electricity installation.7  
2. **Mutual Consent**: If the existing consumer (e.g., a parent gifting to a child) is alive, their signature on the NOC is mandatory. In case of disputes during partition where consent is withheld, the applicant can proceed by providing an Indemnity Bond along with a court-verified partition decree.7  
3. **Security Deposit Allocation**: In partition deeds, the distribution of utility deposits should ideally be mentioned. If not, the new applicant may be required to pay a fresh security deposit if the old one cannot be apportioned.24

## **Fee Structure and Security Deposit Jurisprudence**

The financial obligations during a name change are not limited to the administrative fee but also include the maintenance of the Security Deposit.

### **Administrative Service Fees**

The official fees for processing a name change are relatively nominal and are determined by the load and voltage of the installation.4

* **Low Tension (LT) Installations (Domestic/Commercial)**: ₹100 per installation.4  
* **High Tension (HT) Installations**: ₹500 per installation.7

### **Security Deposit (MMD) Regulations**

Every consumer must maintain a Security Deposit with BESCOM, calculated as the **Monthly Minimum Deposit (MMD)**. This amount is equivalent to two months of average consumption for monthly billing cycles or three months for bi-monthly cycles.21

During a name change, the applicant has two options regarding this deposit:

1. **Deposit Transfer**: If the previous owner signs the consent letter (NOC) and agrees to transfer the deposit, the existing amount in the BESCOM ledger is moved to the new owner’s name. This is the most cost-effective method.8  
2. **Fresh Deposit Payment**: If the previous owner cannot be traced or refuses to transfer the deposit, the new applicant must pay the full MMD amount based on current tariff rates. The previous owner can then apply for a refund of their original deposit separately.24

### **Dues and Arrears Policy**

The KERC (Conditions of Supply) strictly mandates that "there shall be no arrears outstanding against the installation" at the time of transfer.7 However, the transferee is required to sign an undertaking to pay any outstanding dues or "short-claims" that may be detected later relating to the period prior to the transfer.11 This ensures that the utility's revenue is protected even if the consumer identity changes.

## **Processing Timelines: SLA vs. Actual Delivery**

In Bangalore, the processing of government services is governed by the **Sakala scheme**, which sets statutory deadlines for officials.

### **Official Service Level Agreement (SLA)**

Under the Sakala Act, the "Indicative Maximum Time Limit" for the transfer of installation is **7 working days** from the date of receipt of a complete application and the requisite fees.7 If the department fails to provide the service or reject it with valid reasons within this period, the applicant has the right to file an appeal with the designated officer.9

### **Operation Realities and Delays**

While the law mandates 7 days, the practical timeline in Bangalore sub-divisions typically ranges from **15 to 30 days**.4 The discrepancy is often due to:

* **Physical Verification Latency**: The Junior Engineer may take 3-5 days to conduct a physical site visit or verify the meter photo against the master ledger.9  
* **Administrative Backlog**: Files must move physically through several desks (Case Worker, Superintendent, AE, and AEE) before the final signature is affixed.4  
* **System Integration**: Updates in the R-APDRP system are often batch-processed, meaning changes made on the 10th of the month might only reflect on the bill generated on the 1st of the following month.15

## **Common Rejection and Delay Reasons**

Understanding the common pitfalls can help in ensuring a first-time successful application. Legal knowledge base users should note the following frequent triggers for file return or rejection:

1. **Signature Discrepancy**: The signature of the seller on the NOC must match their signature on the Sale Deed or the original BESCOM records. Significant mismatches (often cited as \>10% variation) lead to rejection.4  
2. **Incomplete Power Supply Agreement**: The PSA must be executed in duplicate, and every page must be signed by the applicant. Missing initials on any page are a common cause for delay.7  
3. **Illegible Meter Photos**: If the serial number or the "RR Number" on the meter box is not clearly visible in the photo printout, the JE will refuse to certify the report.9  
4. **Pending Interest/Arrears**: Often, consumers pay the "bill amount" but miss the "arrears" or "interest" column in the bill. The system will flag even a ₹1 balance, blocking the transfer.4  
5. **Address Mismatch**: If the property address on the Sale Deed differs from the electricity bill (e.g., due to changes in ward numbers or road names), a separate "Address Correction" or "Shifting" application may be required.9  
6. **Khata Requirements**: While not strictly mandatory in all sub-divisions, many AEEs insist on a Khata Certificate in the new owner's name as primary proof of municipal recognition.6

## **Legal and Practical Consequences of Non-Compliance**

The decision to leave an electricity connection in the name of a previous owner or a deceased person is fraught with risks that can manifest in legal and financial complications.

### **Loss of Statutory Protections and Benefits**

As noted earlier, the **Gruha Jyothi Scheme** is the most immediate loss. Beyond this, the government often launches "one-time settlement" (OTS) schemes for waiving interest on delayed payments; these schemes usually require the applicant to be the registered consumer.4

### **Jurisprudential Risks in Property Disputes**

In Indian property litigation, utility bills are frequently produced in court as evidence of "settled possession." If a previous owner or their heirs harbor ill intent, a bill in their name can be used to falsely claim that they have not handed over possession or that they are still utilizing the property.5 Legally, having the utility records in the current owner's name creates a "stronger legal position" in case of encroachment or title challenges.5

### **Insurance and Financial Hurdles**

* **Repudiation of Claims**: If an electrical short circuit causes a fire, the insurance company may reject the claim if the policy is in the new owner's name but the utility contract is in the old owner's name, citing a lack of "insurable interest" or "compliance with safety norms".5  
* **Loan Impediments**: Banks conducting an annual review of home loans or processing top-up loans require the latest utility bills in the borrower's name. Outdated bills can trigger a "technical default" or "documentation deficiency" flag.5  
* **Account Termination**: BESCOM reserves the right to terminate the agreement and disconnect supply if they find the "Registered Consumer" is no longer the owner or occupier. Under Clause 36.00, the transfer must be initiated "forthwith" upon the change of ownership.7

## **Final Synthesis and Recommendations**

The process of BESCOM name change in Bangalore is a vital administrative task that bridges the gap between a private property transaction and the public utility record. To ensure seamless compliance, stakeholders should adhere to the following strategic recommendations:

* **Prioritize the Offline Route**: Despite the availability of online portals, the "Hybrid-Offline" approach—where documents are prepared digitally but submitted physically—remains the most reliable way to handle the technical verification by the Junior Engineer.9  
* **Synchronize Property Documents**: Before initiating the BESCOM transfer, ensure that the Khata Transfer with the BBMP is complete. Having a Khata in the new owner's name significantly reduces the friction at the BESCOM sub-division office.14  
* **Maintain Photographic Evidence**: Consumers should take a timestamped photo of their meter reading at the exact time of application to prevent disputes regarding "consumption in transit" between the old and new ownership.9  
* **Utilize Sakala Protections**: Always demand the GSC acknowledgment. It is the only legal tool available to a citizen to hold BESCOM accountable for delays exceeding the 7-day SLA.9

For the legal knowledge base, it is concluded that while the transfer fee of ₹100 is nominal, the cost of non-compliance—ranging from the loss of monthly subsidies to potential title disputes—is substantial. Proper documentation, notarization of deeds, and execution of the prescribed Indemnity Bonds are the three pillars of a legally sound installation transfer in the Bangalore electricity supply ecosystem.
