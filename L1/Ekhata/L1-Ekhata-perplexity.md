1. Legal definition and standing of E-Khata; difference from physical Khata, A-Khata and B-Khata

1.1. Statutory concept of “Khata” under the Karnataka Municipal Corporations Act, 1976 (KMC Act)

1. Khata is not defined as a separate instrument in the KMC Act, but flows from the statutory scheme for assessment and collection of property tax by municipal corporations, including BBMP.ezylegal+1

2. Sections 108, 108A, 109, 112, 112A, 112B and 112D of the KMC Act (as amended) require the corporation to levy property tax on all buildings/lands within city limits, prepare and publish a property tax register, and survey lands and buildings and prepare a property register.indiacode+1

3. Section 114 imposes an “obligation of transferor and transferee to give notice of transfer” of property so that the municipal property register can be updated, while section 114A (inserted by the 1995 Amendment) allows the Commissioner to reopen and review transfers got recorded by fraud or misrepresentation.legitquest+1

4. Courts and practice recognise that the “Khata” (Khata certificate and extract) is the practical manifestation of entries in the property tax and property registers for a particular property and owner, evidencing assessment and tax liability under the Act.hemanthassociates+1

1.2. What E-Khata is (concept and legal standing)

1. BBMP’s e‑Aasthi portal states that a “Draft eKhata has been issued as per existing BBMP Property Tax Register” and that citizens must “upload additional information for getting Final eKhata Online,” indicating that e‑Khata is a digitised, e‑signed version of the same Khata record maintained under the KMC Act.\[[bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov](https://bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov.in/CitzLogin.aspx)\]​

2. The portal describes that citizens can log in using mobile and OTP, view and download draft e‑Khata, and on submission of prescribed documents obtain “Final eKhata Online,” which replaces the earlier manual (paper) Khata certificate for the same property.\[[bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov](https://bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov.in/CitzLogin.aspx)\]​

3. Banking and housing‑finance literature describes E‑Khata as BBMP’s online property record system in Bengaluru that shows ownership and tax details and is accepted by lenders as equivalent to the earlier physical A‑Khata for compliant properties.ujjivansfb+1

4. Ujjivan Small Finance Bank’s 2025 guide notes that E‑Khata is “the digital counterpart of the traditional khata certificate” and “serves as official proof of property ownership in Karnataka” in the context of municipal records and bank due‑diligence, though like all khata it does not itself create title.courtkutchehry+1

1.3. Legal nature of Khata / E-Khata (not a title document)

1. The Karnataka High Court has repeatedly held that entries in the municipal property register / khata are primarily for property‑tax purposes and are not, by themselves, conclusive proof of title.casemine+1

2. In C. Krishnappa v. Commissioner, Corporation of the City of Bangalore, the Court observed that section 114 prescribes procedure and documents for change of khata, but “entry in the property register in itself is not evidence of title,” and the Commissioner may decline mutation if there is a serious title dispute.\[[courtkutchehry](https://www.courtkutchehry.com/judgements/504271/c-krishnappa-vs-commissioner-corporation-of-the-city-of-ba/)\]​

3. In Sri N. Anil v. The Commissioner, the High Court reiterated that khata is a tax‑assessment record and that disputes over ownership must be resolved in civil court, after which khata can be corrected under sections 114 and 114A.\[[casemine](https://www.casemine.com/judgement/in/61fd80989fca19285e0262ba)\]​

4. Experienced practitioners similarly explain that “khatha issued by BBMP means an account of the property at BBMP” and is required for paying tax, obtaining licences and loans, but is not a substitute for registered title deeds.\[[hemanthassociates](https://hemanthassociates.com/bbmp-katha/)\]​

1.4. A-Khata and its status

1. A‑Khata refers, in BBMP practice, to properties that fully comply with the KMC Act, building by‑laws and zoning regulations and are therefore entered in the primary (“Form A”) property register.brigadegroup+1

2. Such properties typically have valid land conversion (if former revenue land), approved layout or building plan, occupancy certificate where applicable, and no major deviations; they are therefore considered “legal” properties under municipal law.bajajhousingfinance+1

3. Owners of A‑Khata properties are routinely allowed building licences, trade licences, building plan sanctions and can obtain housing loans from banks, which treat an A‑Khata (or its digital E‑Khata equivalent) as an important municipal compliance document.bajajhousingfinance+1

1.5. B-Khata and its legal position

1. BBMP historically maintained a separate “B Register” for properties with irregularities—such as unauthorised layouts, buildings on revenue land without DC conversion, or constructions without sanctioned plans—and issued so‑called “B‑Khata” acknowledgements.archive.newskarnataka+1

2. The Karnataka High Court (Justice Ram Mohan Reddy) has held that the practice of issuing “B‑Khata” is illegal and that B‑Register entries are only acknowledgements for tax collection, not valid khata extracts or recognition of ownership or legality.bangaloremirror.indiatimes+1

3. The Court directed BBMP to include all properties in the Form A register and clarified that “B‑Khata is not an entitlement to the property, but simply an acknowledgement that tax for the said property is being paid… it is not a title deed and is just an identification entered in a register titled ‘B’.”\[[archive.newskarnataka](https://archive.newskarnataka.com/karnataka/bengaluru/bbmp-in-a-fix-high-court-says-b-khatas-are-illegal/25032015)\]​

4. Financial institutions and large developers echo that B‑Khata properties are treated as “unauthorised” and are broadly ineligible for building permits and institutional finance until regularised and converted to A‑Khata.brigadegroup+1

1.6. E-Khata vs physical (manual) Khata, A-Khata and B-Khata

1. Manual (physical) Khata refers to the earlier paper‑based Khata certificate and extract issued by BBMP revenue offices, reflecting entries in the Form A property register.\[[hemanthassociates](https://hemanthassociates.com/bbmp-katha/)\]​

2. E‑Khata is the fully digitised, e‑signed Khata output available through BBMP’s e‑Aasthi portal; it mirrors the same underlying A‑Register data but is stored and delivered electronically.ujjivansfb+1

3. For compliant properties, an E‑Khata is effectively the electronic form of an A‑Khata certificate; BBMP and banking guides clarify that it has the same legal utility for tax, licences and lending, and the civic body is moving towards making E‑Khata the only acceptable format.newskarnataka+1

4. B‑Khata is neither a separate class of E‑Khata nor a recognised khata extract; where such properties are regularised and brought into compliance (DC conversion, approved plan, payment of betterment charges), they must ultimately be entered in the main register and issued an A‑Khata / E‑Khata for full legal usability.nobroker+1

5. Governing body and official portal

2.1. Competent authority

1. Within Bengaluru city, the Bruhat Bengaluru Mahanagara Palike (BBMP) is the municipal corporation responsible for assessment of property tax, maintenance of property registers and issuance of Khata / E‑Khata under the KMC Act.indiacode+1

2. The BBMP e‑Aasthi site identifies “Bengaluru City Corporations – Joint Commissioner of Revenue, NR Square, Bengaluru” with official email dcrev@bbmp.gov.in and central telephone numbers as the controlling authority for E‑Khata.bbmpeaasthi.karnataka+1

3. Day‑to‑day processing of E‑Khata applications is handled by the Revenue wing—Assistant Revenue Officers (AROs), Revenue Inspectors and case‑workers—in the ward and zone where the property lies, under the overall supervision of the Joint Commissioner (Revenue).indianexpress+1

2.2. Official E-Khata / e-Aasthi portal

1. The official portal for BBMP E‑Khata is “BBMP e‑Aasthi”, accessible at [https://bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov.in](https://bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov.in/), which is a Government of Karnataka site maintained by NIC.\[[bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov](https://bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov.in/)\]​

2. The citizen login and E‑Khata functions are at [https://bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov.in/CitzLogin.aspx](https://bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov.in/CitzLogin.aspx), which provides links titled “How to get Final eKhata – English / Kannada,” “FAQs on eKhata,” “eKhatha status based on ePID,” and instructions to log in using mobile and OTP to see/download draft E‑Khata.\[[bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov](https://bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov.in/CitzLogin.aspx)\]​

3. The same page lists the mandatory documents to be uploaded for Final E‑Khata—Registered Deed, Aadhaar‑based eKYC, SAS Property Tax Application Number, Property Photo, documents to prove A‑Khata status, and Encumbrance Certificate where immediate sale/transfer is planned—and provides an official E‑Khata helpline number (9480683695) and email (bbmpekhata@gmail.com).\[[bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov](https://bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov.in/CitzLogin.aspx)\]​

2.3. Related service portals (Sakala / Seva Sindhu / Bangalore One)

1. Karnataka’s Seva Sindhu portal ([https://sevasindhu.karnataka.gov.in/Sevasindhu/English](https://sevasindhu.karnataka.gov.in/Sevasindhu/English)) and the Sakala mission portal ([https://sakala.kar.nic.in](https://sakala.kar.nic.in/)) host various BBMP revenue services—such as Khata transfer and Khata certificate/extract issuance—under the Karnataka Sakala Services Act, 2011, which guarantees time‑bound delivery of listed services.sevasindhu.karnataka+2

2. The Bengaluru Urban district site notes that Seva Sindhu is integrated with Bangalore One and other citizen centres, allowing citizens to access BBMP services, including property‑tax related services, through nearby centres as well as online.\[[bengaluruurban.nic](https://bengaluruurban.nic.in/en/service/seva-sindhu/)\]​

3. A Kannada report confirms that E‑Khata services are also available in assisted mode at Bangalore One centres, where centre staff submit the online application on behalf of citizens, subject to BBMP’s prescribed fees and additional centre service charges.\[[vijaykarnataka](https://vijaykarnataka.com/news/bengaluru-city/bbmp-e-khata-available-at-bangalore-one-centers-too-what-documents-are-required-how-much-is-the-fee/articleshow/115206105.cms)\]​

4. Official step-by-step process for E-Khata (BBMP / Government guidelines)

3.1. Overview of the two main scenarios

1. BBMP distinguishes between (a) properties which already exist in the BBMP property‑tax register and have a “draft e‑Khata” generated, and (b) properties which do not yet have even a manual Khata and require a “New Khata” application.\[[bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov](https://bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov.in/CitzLogin.aspx)\]​

2. For most existing BBMP‑taxed properties, the process is to log into e‑Aasthi, locate the draft e‑Khata, verify details, upload mandated documents and obtain the Final E‑Khata online; for properties without earlier khata, citizens are directed to a separate “How to apply for New Khata if don’t have even manual Khata” flow on e‑Aasthi or through Sakala/Seva Sindhu.nobroker+1

3.2. Process for properties with a draft E-Khata (standard case)

Step 1 – Prepare documents

1. As per BBMP e‑Aasthi instructions, citizens must have ready: registered sale/gift/partition deed, Aadhaar for all owners (for eKYC), SAS Property Tax Application Number, latest tax paid receipt, property photograph, and documents to prove A‑Khata status (such as earlier Khata or approvals).vijaykarnataka+1

2. BBMP’s own video guidance (linked from the portal and reported in multiple guides) also recommends having BESCOM electricity bill, Encumbrance Certificate (EC), and a photograph / GPS screenshot of the house or flat to upload.\[[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6EJbGN_pKA8)\]​\[[ujjivansfb.bank](https://www.ujjivansfb.bank.in/banking-blogs/home-loan/bbmp-e-khata-bangalore-online-registration-guide)\]​

Step 2 – Access the e-Aasthi citizen portal

1. Go to [https://bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov.in](https://bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov.in/) and choose the citizen login page, which offers Kannada and English interfaces.bbmpeaasthi.karnataka+1

2. On the CitzLogin page, enter your mobile number and obtain an OTP to sign in; the system records that “Login using mobile & OTP” is required to see and download any draft E‑Khata.\[[bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov](https://bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov.in/CitzLogin.aspx)\]​

Step 3 – Locate the draft E-Khata

1. After login, use the links “Click here for draft eKhata if you know your ward” or “Click here to know your ward & see draft eKhatha” to navigate to the ward‑wise draft E‑Khata listing.\[[bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov](https://bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov.in/CitzLogin.aspx)\]​

2. The Indian Express and other explainers state that citizens must identify their ward name and number from the BBMP property‑tax receipt, then download the draft E‑Khata generated from the existing property‑tax register.indianexpress+1

Step 4 – Verify and correct draft details

1. The draft E‑Khata shows owner name, property address, area, PID/EPID number and other particulars pulled from BBMP’s tax register; citizens must verify these details against their registered deed and tax receipts.\[[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6EJbGN_pKA8)\]​\[[bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov](https://bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov.in/CitzLogin.aspx)\]​

2. If names are misspelt or earlier owner names still appear, BBMP guidance and citizen‑experience reports indicate that such corrections can be requested through the portal or by filing objections / correction requests at the ARO office, but title disputes cannot be resolved in this process and require civil adjudication.\[[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=050y2c3EgOg)\]​zencitizen+1

Step 5 – Upload mandatory documents and complete eKYC

1. The CitzLogin page sets out that for Final E‑Khata, citizens must upload: Registered Deed, Aadhaar‑based eKYC, SAS Property Tax Application Number, Property Photo, documents to prove A‑Khata, and EC where immediate sale/transfer is planned.\[[bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov](https://bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov.in/CitzLogin.aspx)\]​

2. The Indian Express 2024 guideline for urgent E‑Khata finalisation further requires an EC valid from the day before the registration date up to the date of application, and Aadhaar‑based eKYC for all owners; non‑Aadhaar owners (such as NRIs) must authenticate identity at the ARO office with alternate ID documents (passport, voter ID, driving licence).\[[indianexpress](https://indianexpress.com/article/cities/bangalore/bbmp-urgent-e-khata-finalisation-amid-property-registrations-9627274/)\]​

Step 6 – Submit application and pay the E-Khata fee

1. Once documents are uploaded, the citizen completes a declaration and e‑signs (usually using Aadhaar e‑sign), after which the system generates an application / ePID and demands payment of the statutory E‑Khata document fee.\[[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6EJbGN_pKA8)\]​\[[bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov](https://bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov.in/CitzLogin.aspx)\]​

2. BBMP and media reports specify that the official fee for issuance of the final E‑Khata document is ₹125 per property, payable online via e‑Aasthi or at Bangalore One centres; any larger amounts demanded by intermediaries are explicitly discouraged by BBMP.deccanherald+2

Step 7 – Scrutiny and verification by BBMP

1. BBMP officials verify the application details against the e‑Aasthi property register, Kaveri registration data and BESCOM records, and may carry out field inspection, particularly where there are deviations, B‑Register history or inconsistencies.indianexpress+1

2. Ujjivan’s summary and citizen‑group documentation explain that if documents match the records, the ARO can approve the Final E‑Khata entirely online; otherwise, the application may be sent back for clarification or for in‑person review at the ARO office.zencitizen+1

Step 8 – Download Final E-Khata and track status

1. Once approved and after fee payment, citizens can use the “eKhatha status based on ePID” and the “How to get Final eKhata” links on e‑Aasthi to check status and download the final, digitally signed E‑Khata certificate.\[[bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov](https://bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov.in/CitzLogin.aspx)\]​

2. BBMP and bank guides note that the final E‑Khata remains available for download on the portal and can be printed as needed, functioning as the official khata certificate for future transactions.brigadegroup+1

3.3. Process where there is no prior manual Khata (New Khata)

1. The e‑Aasthi CitzLogin page provides a dedicated link “How to apply for New Khata if don’t have even manual Khata – Click here,” covering cases where properties have registered deeds but are not yet in BBMP’s Khata registers.\[[bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov](https://bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov.in/CitzLogin.aspx)\]​

2. For such new Khata registration or Khata transfer (after sale, gift, inheritance, etc.), standard practice—as collected in legal and tax guides—is to apply through Sakala / Seva Sindhu or at the BBMP ARO office, submit all title documents, pay the statutory Khata registration fee (see Section 5 below) and await mutation into the property register, after which an E‑Khata can be obtained.cleartax+2

3. The Economic Times, reporting on Sakala’s intervention, notes that BBMP has been directed to issue new Khatas for individual flats within 30 days of application under the Sakala framework, although this relates to Khata registration generally and not specifically to the newer E‑Khata workflow.\[[economictimes](https://economictimes.com/news/india/sakala-effect-bbmp-will-now-issue-new-khatas-for-individual-flats-in-30-days/articleshow/83544208.cms)\]​

3.4. Assisted mode via Bangalore One and Seva Sindhu

1. Citizens who are not comfortable with online processes may visit Bangalore One centres, where staff access the e‑Aasthi portal and submit the E‑Khata application on their behalf.bengaluruurban.nic+1

2. A Kannada report states that at Bangalore One centres, citizens pay a service fee of ₹45 plus ₹5 per page for scanning documents, in addition to the ₹125 E‑Khata fee payable to BBMP when the final E‑Khata is ready.\[[vijaykarnataka](https://vijaykarnataka.com/news/bengaluru-city/bbmp-e-khata-available-at-bangalore-one-centers-too-what-documents-are-required-how-much-is-the-fee/articleshow/115206105.cms)\]​

3. For traditional Khata transfer / Khata certificate and extract, NoBroker and other guides describe a Seva Sindhu / Sakala flow: citizens log into the Sakala portal, select the relevant Khata service, upload documents, e‑sign, obtain a Sakala number and track status until the E‑signed Khata certificate/extract is ready for download.nobroker+1

3.5. Objections, corrections and appeals

1. e‑Aasthi explicitly allows citizens to “file Objection not to issue Final eKhata as well on any property,” enabling neighbours or interested persons to object to issuance if they believe the applicant has no valid title or there are errors.\[[bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov](https://bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov.in/CitzLogin.aspx)\]​

2. Where E‑Khata has been wrongly issued or obtained by fraud, section 114A of the KMC Act empowers the Commissioner to review and reopen a recorded transfer within three years if it was procured by fraud, misrepresentation or suppression of facts, after giving the affected person a hearing.\[[legitquest](https://www.legitquest.com/act/karnataka-municipal-corporations-amendment-act-1995/6e99)\]​

3. Errors in names, dimensions or other particulars (without title dispute) are typically handled through correction applications under BBMP’s internal instructions or limited review powers, as described in practitioner and citizen‑group guides on E‑Khata corrections.ezylegal+1

4. Complete document checklist (BBMP E-Khata – Bangalore)

4.1. Core mandatory documents (as per e-Aasthi and official guidelines)

1. Registered Deed:

   * Registered sale deed, gift deed, partition deed, settlement deed or other conveyance under the Registration Act, 1908, covering the property and in favour of the current applicant(s).ezylegal+1

2. Identity and eKYC:

   * Aadhaar card for each owner for Aadhaar‑based eKYC (mandatory where available).indianexpress+1

   * For NRIs or persons without Aadhaar, passport, voter ID or driving licence may be used for in‑person verification at the ARO office.\[[indianexpress](https://indianexpress.com/article/cities/bangalore/bbmp-urgent-e-khata-finalisation-amid-property-registrations-9627274/)\]​

3. Property tax details:

   * SAS Property Tax Application Number (10‑digit or similar, as per BBMP receipt).\[[bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov](https://bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov.in/CitzLogin.aspx)\]​

   * Latest property‑tax paid receipt (typically last assessment year, and in some cases last 3–5 years), showing no arrears.sobha+1

4. Property identification:

   * Property photograph (front view, showing structure).\[[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6EJbGN_pKA8)\]​\[[bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov](https://bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov.in/CitzLogin.aspx)\]​

   * Property Identification (PID / EPID) number where allotted by BBMP.\[[sobha](https://www.sobha.com/blog/bbmp-e-khata-bangalore-online-registration/)\]​

3. Fee structure and payment modes

5.1. Statutory Khata registration / transfer fees (mutation)

1. For new Khata registration or Khata transfer (i.e., mutation after sale, gift, inheritance etc.), multiple legal‑tax guides state that BBMP charges a Khata registration / transfer fee of 2% of the property value mentioned in the sale deed or, more precisely, 2% of the stamp‑duty amount paid on the registered instrument, subject to a minimum.cleartax+1

2. One widely‑cited explanation is that the “Khata registration fee \[is\] 2% of the property value as mentioned in the sale deed,” paid to BBMP after assessment, and that this is distinct from property tax; this is consistent with ClearTax’s summary of BBMP practice.\[[cleartax](https://cleartax.in/s/khatha-registration-khatha-transfer)\]​

3. NoBroker’s legal forum clarifies BBMP practice as “an administrative charge \= 2% of the stamp duty amount on the registered deed, with a minimum of ₹500,” and notes that this applies even in inheritance‑based Khata transfers.\[[nobroker](https://www.nobroker.in/forum/what-are-the-charges-for-making-a-khata-transfer/)\]​

4. Additional “improvement charges” (sometimes called betterment charges) of approximately ₹200–₹250 per square metre in certain BBMP areas may apply during registration or conversion of B‑Khata to A‑Khata, depending on location and regularisation scheme.bricknbolt+1

5.2. E-Khata document fee (Final E-Khata)

1. BBMP has fixed an official fee of ₹125 for issuance of the Final E‑Khata document per property, whether applied directly online or via assisted centres; this is characterised as a document‑issuance fee, separate from Khata registration/mutation charges.constrofacilitator+2

2. A Deccan Herald 2025 report explicitly notes that “the official fee, set by BBMP, is just Rs 125” for the final E‑Khata, while highlighting that some officials and agents attempt to charge much higher unofficial sums.\[[deccanherald](https://www.deccanherald.com/india/karnataka/bengaluru/e-khata-gets-pricey-for-some-residents-despite-review-in-bengaluru-3446128)\]​

Kannada media reporting on Bangalore One centres states that citizens must pay ₹125 to BBMP towards E‑Khata, over and above centre service charges, once the final E‑Khata is ready for printing.\[[vijaykarnataka](https://vijaykarnataka.com/news/bengaluru-city/bbmp-e-khata-available-at-bangalore-one-centers-too-what-documents-are-required-how-much-is-the-fee/articleshow/115206105.cms)\]​

3. Processing timeline – official SLA vs real-world experience

6.1. Formal timelines and Sakala context

1. Under the Karnataka Sakala Services Act, 2011, notified services must be delivered within stipulated time limits; failure can lead to compensatory costs and departmental action, but the Act does not itself list E‑Khata—it only provides the framework for time‑bound services.\[[sakala.kar.nic](https://sakala.kar.nic.in/Service%20Compendium/01-12-2022%20sakala_compendium_English%20\(Final%20file\).pdf)\]​

2. Economic Times reporting on Sakala’s intervention notes that BBMP has been directed to issue new Khatas for individual flats within 30 days of application, under a re‑engineered process meant to avoid delays caused by needing all flat owners to apply together.\[[economictimes](https://economictimes.com/news/india/sakala-effect-bbmp-will-now-issue-new-khatas-for-individual-flats-in-30-days/articleshow/83544208.cms)\]​

3. However, citizen documentation and advocacy reports explicitly state that the newer E‑Khata service is currently not covered under Sakala, meaning no statutory time guarantee applies specifically to E‑Khata issuance.\[[zencitizen](https://zencitizen.in/2024/10/04/what-to-expect-when-you-are-getting-an-ekhata/)\]​

6.2. Timelines stated in recent BBMP / bank communications

1. Ujjivan’s 2025 E‑Khata guide states that “Delivery Time: 15 Days” is the expected timeline for E‑Khata issuance and further quotes BBMP’s Special Commissioner (Revenue) as saying that the final E‑Khata will be processed and issued within two days of application submission in straightforward cases.\[[ujjivansfb.bank](https://www.ujjivansfb.bank.in/banking-blogs/home-loan/bbmp-e-khata-bangalore-online-registration-guide)\]​

2. Indian Express, in the context of urgent property registrations scheduled in October 2024, reports that BBMP issued special guidelines to fast‑track final E‑Khatas for such cases through an online process via e‑Aasthi, indicating an expectation of completion within days when documentation and Kaveri data align.\[[indianexpress](https://indianexpress.com/article/cities/bangalore/bbmp-urgent-e-khata-finalisation-amid-property-registrations-9627274/)\]​

6.3. Real-world timelines

1. Citizen‑group research (ZenCitizen, 2024\) notes that although BBMP provides a nominal timeline of about seven days for E‑Khata issuance once all documents are correctly uploaded, in practice approvals often take significantly longer due to technical glitches, repeated rejections and the need to meet AROs in person.\[[zencitizen](https://zencitizen.in/2024/10/04/what-to-expect-when-you-are-getting-an-ekhata/)\]​

2. Media and citizen reports indicate that issuance can range from a few days in simple, fully‑compliant flat cases (especially where the builder has coordinated with BBMP) to several weeks or even months where there are B‑register histories, incomplete tax records or clerical mismatches.vaultproptech+1

6.4. Stage-wise indicative timeline (Bangalore, BBMP)

1. For a standard, compliant apartment unit with existing draft E‑Khata and no title issues:

   * Upload and e‑submission: same day;

   * Internal verification and approval: 2–7 working days (as per BBMP and bank guidance);

   * Final E‑Khata downloadable soon after fee payment.zencitizen+1

2. For new Khata / mutation cases after sale or inheritance, where Sakala‑style processes apply but E‑Khata is the final output:

   * Khata registration / transfer (mutation) under Sakala: directed to be completed within about 30 days;

   * E‑Khata generation thereafter: a few days once mutation is recorded.economictimes+1

3. Legal provisions and circulars relevant to E-Khata

7.1. Key KMC Act provisions

1. Section 108 / 108A – Description and class of property tax; 108A specifically sets out the framework for property‑tax levy and calculation for Bruhat Bengaluru Mahanagara Palike, tying BBMP’s tax regime (and thus its property registers) directly to the Act.mohua+1

2. Section 109 – Method of assessment of property tax, including assessment of taxable capital value of buildings and lands; this underlies the property‑tax entries that are later expressed in Khata and E‑Khata.mohua+1

3. Sections 112, 112A, 112B and 112D – Provide that property tax is payable by owners/occupiers, that every liable person must submit returns, that a property‑tax register must be prepared and published, and that there must be a survey of lands and buildings and preparation of a property register.\[[indiacode.nic](https://www.indiacode.nic.in/bitstream/123456789/8129/1/14_of_1977_\(e\).pdf)\]​

4. Section 113 – Governs demand for payment of property tax and appeals against such demand, further underlining that khata entries primarily evidence tax assessment.\[[indiacode.nic](https://www.indiacode.nic.in/bitstream/123456789/8129/1/14_of_1977_\(e\).pdf)\]​

5. Section 114 – “Obligation of transferor and transferee to give notice of transfer,” requiring both parties to notify the Commissioner of transfers of properties within corporation limits so that the property register can be updated and tax levied on the correct person; practice‑oriented guides emphasise that application for Khata transfer must be made within 90 days of sale / transfer / death of the khatedar.propseva+1

6. Section 114A – Inserted by the 1995 Amendment, empowers the Commissioner to review any transfer of title recorded under section 114 that was obtained by fraud, misrepresentation, suppression of facts or by furnishing false or incomplete material, within three years, after hearing the affected party.\[[legitquest](https://www.legitquest.com/act/karnataka-municipal-corporations-amendment-act-1995/6e99)\]​

7.2. Judicial guidance on Khata and B-Khata

1. As noted earlier, decisions like C. Krishnappa and Sri N. Anil clarify that khata is not conclusive proof of title, though it is important evidence and a precondition for tax and certain civic services.courtkutchehry+1

2. High Court orders in 2013–2015 (summarised in media like NewsKarnataka) have declared the practice of issuing “B‑Khata” illegal and directed BBMP to maintain a unified property register (Form A), treating B‑Register entries merely as tax documents, not as legal Khata extracts.\[[archive.newskarnataka](https://archive.newskarnataka.com/karnataka/bengaluru/bbmp-in-a-fix-high-court-says-b-khatas-are-illegal/25032015)\]​

7.3. Government circulars and policy signals on E-Khata

1. While underlying E‑Khata circulars are not all publicly indexed, press reports and bank/legal summaries cite BBMP circulars that:

   * Authorised generation of ward‑wise draft E‑Khatas for all properties in the property‑tax register;

   * Introduced the ₹125 fee for final E‑Khata; and

   * Empowered resident welfare associations to conduct E‑Khata “melas” with BBMP support.constrofacilitator+1

2. A 2024 Indian Express report details BBMP’s guideline for “urgent e‑Khata finalisation” ahead of property registrations, requiring specific EC validity, Aadhaar‑based eKYC for all owners, and use of the e‑Aasthi portal to complete the process before scheduled registration dates, reflecting a formalised process though the underlying circular is not annexed.\[[indianexpress](https://indianexpress.com/article/cities/bangalore/bbmp-urgent-e-khata-finalisation-amid-property-registrations-9627274/)\]​

7.4. Sakala / Seva Sindhu framework

1. The Karnataka Sakala Services Act, 2011 and its service compendium create a right for citizens to obtain notified services within stipulated time limits, with penal costs for officials delaying services, and many BBMP Khata services (Khata registration, Khata extract) have been brought under this framework.sakala.kar.nic+1

2. However, citizen‑advocacy documentation confirms that the specific E‑Khata service on e‑Aasthi is not currently listed under Sakala, so statutory time guarantees do not apply directly to E‑Khata, although BBMP’s own internal targets (e.g., 2–7 days) are often cited.\[[zencitizen](https://zencitizen.in/2024/10/04/what-to-expect-when-you-are-getting-an-ekhata/)\]​

3. Common reasons for rejection or delay of E-Khata applications

4. Mismatch between BBMP records and registered deed:

   * Differences in dimensions, survey numbers, flat numbers, or owner names between the e‑Aasthi draft and the registered deed commonly lead to scrutiny, queries or rejection until corrected, as repeatedly highlighted in citizen‑group and media reports.deccanherald+1

5. Incomplete or outdated tax compliance:

   * Non‑payment or under‑payment of property tax, or absence of the latest tax receipt tied to the correct SAS Application Number, often results in applications being kept pending or rejected until arrears are cleared.nobroker+1

6. Unclear title / chain of documents:

   * Where the chain of title documents is incomplete or contested (e.g., competing claims, missing link deeds), AROs may refuse mutation or E‑Khata issuance and ask parties to resolve disputes in civil court, consistent with High Court guidance on section 114.casemine+1

7. B-Register / unauthorised status:

   * Properties on revenue land without DC conversion, unauthorised layouts or constructions without approved plans/OC may remain in B‑Register, and E‑Khata requests can be rejected or kept pending until regularisation (betterment charges, conversion orders, etc.) is completed.archive.newskarnataka+1

8. Document deficiencies:

   * Failure to upload all required documents—such as the registered deed, all pages of the document, EC where insisted upon (especially for urgent registrations), or co‑owners’ Aadhaar/eKYC—regularly triggers “re‑upload” messages or outright rejection.zencitizen+2

9. Aadhaar / eKYC issues:

   * Name mismatches between Aadhaar and sale deed, non‑availability of Aadhaar for some joint owners, or technical errors in e‑signing can block submission or cause later refusal, particularly in a system anchored on Aadhaar‑based eKYC.\[[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6EJbGN_pKA8)\]​\[[indianexpress](https://indianexpress.com/article/cities/bangalore/bbmp-urgent-e-khata-finalisation-amid-property-registrations-9627274/)\]​

10. Incorrect categorisation of case:

    * Applying for “new E‑Khata” when a manual A‑Khata already exists, or choosing the wrong option for building approvals / mutation status, can cause the system to treat the case as fresh mutation and demand mutation fees again, leading to confusion and delays.reddit+1

11. Technical and portal issues:

    * Citizen accounts highlight frequent portal downtime, missing draft E‑Khatas for some B‑Khata or newly added properties, and inconsistent behaviour of the helplines, which can stall otherwise complete applications.timesofindia.indiatimes+2

12. Suspected irregularity / need for field inspection:

    * Large deviations from sanctioned plans, disputed parking areas, or complaints from neighbours may lead AROs to insist on site inspections, during which applications may remain pending beyond nominal timelines.\[[youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=050y2c3EgOg)\]​\[[deccanherald](https://www.deccanherald.com/india/karnataka/bengaluru/e-khata-gets-pricey-for-some-residents-despite-review-in-bengaluru-3446128)\]​

2. When E-Khata is mandatory or practically non-negotiable (BBMP – Bangalore)

10.1. Statutory obligations under the KMC Act

1. Under section 114 of the KMC Act, transferor and transferee are obliged to give notice of transfer within prescribed time (commonly understood in practice as within 90 days) so that the corporation can mutate the property into the transferee’s name; in BBMP, this is operationalised through Khata / E‑Khata transfer.propseva+1

2. While the Act does not use the term “E‑Khata,” once BBMP has migrated to an electronic system, fulfilment of this statutory obligation effectively requires ensuring that the E‑Khata is updated into the transferee’s name for compliant properties.ujjivansfb+1

10.2. Property registration (sale, gift, etc.)

1. As per 2024–2025 reports, BBMP has made E‑Khata mandatory for all property registrations within BBMP limits from 30 September 2024, meaning that sub‑registrar offices will insist on E‑Khata compliance for properties under BBMP jurisdiction at the time of registration.newskarnataka+1

2. Indian Express’ urgent E‑Khata guideline further shows that, in practice, where a registration is scheduled, property owners must finalise E‑Khata (including EC and eKYC) before the registration date to avoid rejection or postponement at the sub‑registrar’s office.\[[indianexpress](https://indianexpress.com/article/cities/bangalore/bbmp-urgent-e-khata-finalisation-amid-property-registrations-9627274/)\]​

10.3. Building plan sanctions, occupancy certificates and modifications

1. A‑Khata (and now its E‑Khata equivalent) is required to obtain or regularise building plan sanctions and occupancy certificates for properties within BBMP limits; properties with only B‑Register entries are treated as unauthorised and cannot generally obtain such approvals until regularised.bajajhousingfinance+2

2. For additional floors, regularisation schemes or amalgamation/bifurcation of properties, BBMP expects the underlying Khata/E‑Khata to correctly reflect ownership and dimensions before processing building‑licence or plan‑modification applications.nobroker+1

10.4. Loans, mortgages and financial transactions

1. Banks and housing‑finance institutions uniformly require Khata certificate / E‑Khata for processing home loans or mortgages on BBMP properties; Bajaj Housing Finance and others state that only A‑Khata / E‑Khata properties are treated as fully compliant and eligible for such finance.bajajhousingfinance+1

2. E‑Khata is thus practically mandatory for accessing institutional finance in Bengaluru for properties under BBMP, as lenders seldom proceed on the basis of B‑Khata or mere tax receipts alone.bajajhousingfinance+1

10.5. Utility connections and civic permissions

1. Property‑law and real‑estate guides observe that Khata certificates and extracts are required for obtaining new water connections, BESCOM electricity connections and for certain government licences within city limits; with digitisation, E‑Khata now fulfils this role.nobroker+1

2. Without Khata / E‑Khata, installation of civic utilities or processing of trade licences can be refused or kept pending, making the document effectively non‑negotiable even where not expressly mandated in separate utility regulations.hemanthassociates+1

10.6. Tax compliance and enforcement

1. BBMP uses Khata/E‑Khata as the basis for assessing and enforcing property tax; failure to secure mutation into the current owner’s name can lead to tax notices being issued in the previous owner’s name, complicating enforcement and exposing parties to penalties.indiacode+1

2. From a compliance perspective, keeping E‑Khata updated is therefore essential to ensure correct attribution of tax liability and to avoid disputes or complications in future transactions, even where there is no immediate sale or loan.ezylegal+1

10.7. Secondary-market transactions and due diligence

1. In Bengaluru practice, buyers, lawyers and banks treat a clear A‑Khata / E‑Khata as a baseline requirement for purchasing BBMP‑governed properties, and properties without it are usually discounted, considered high‑risk or avoided entirely.brigadegroup+1

2. Consequently, while the underlying statute speaks only of property registers and tax, E‑Khata has become a de‑facto mandatory document in almost every significant transaction involving immovable property within BBMP limits—especially after its linkage with property registrations from late 2024 onwards.newskarnataka+1
