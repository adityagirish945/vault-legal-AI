# **Registry of Property in Karnataka: An Authoritative Reference for Legal Knowledge Bases**

The governance of immovable property in Karnataka is an intricate ecosystem where colonial-era statutes intersect with 21st-century digital transformation. The primary legislative pillars—the Registration Act, 1908, and the Karnataka Stamp Act, 1957—provide the structural mandate for property documentation, ensuring that the transfer of rights is conducted with transparency and permanence.1 In recent years, the Government of Karnataka has fundamentally restructured this process through the introduction of Kaveri 2.0, an advanced online property registration system that mandates pre-registration data entry, digital fee payment, and biometric authentication.4 This shift represents a move from a "manual ledger" system to an integrated data environment where property records, tax assessments, and survey maps are digitally cross-referenced to prevent fraud and litigation.4

## **1\. Legal Definition and Foundational Importance**

Property registration is the official recording of an instrument with an authorized public official, specifically the Sub-Registrar of the jurisdictional district.1 Under Section 17 of the Registration Act, 1908, registration is the process by which a document becomes legally effective for the transfer, limitation, or extinguishment of rights in immovable property.1 It is not merely a bureaucratic formality but a cornerstone of property law that establishes the "Doctrine of Public Notice," informing the community at large of any legal interests affecting a specific parcel of land.1

### **1.1 Legal Establishment and Title Sanity**

Under Karnataka law, property registration serves three primary objectives: the perpetuation of documents that may be of legal importance, the prevention of fraud through identity verification, and the creation of a public record that provides a conclusive guarantee of the genuineness of a transaction.1 However, recent jurisprudence from the Supreme Court of India has clarified a critical nuance: registration only records a transaction and does not, in itself, confer or validate a title if the transferor lacked the legal right to sell.12 Thus, while registration is mandatory, it must be supported by an unbroken chain of title and physical possession to constitute full ownership.12

### **1.2 Statutory Importance**

The importance of registration is underscored by Section 49 of the Registration Act, 1908, which mandates that any document required by law to be registered but left unregistered shall not affect the immovable property comprised therein, nor be received as evidence of any transaction affecting such property in a court of law.1 This renders unregistered sale deeds virtually useless for proving ownership in legal disputes.1

| **Public Notice** | The legal presumption that everyone has knowledge of registered property transactions.1 |
| **Admissibility** | The requirement that only registered deeds can be used as evidence of title in litigation.1 |

## **2\. Mandatory Scenarios for Registration**

The necessity for registration is triggered by the nature of the document and the value of the interest it purports to create. In Karnataka, most instruments transferring interests in immovable property valued at ₹100 or more require compulsory registration.1

### **2.1 Sale Deeds and Gift Deeds**

The Sale Deed is the primary conveyance document for the absolute transfer of ownership. In Karnataka, no sale of immovable property is legally recognized without a registered Sale Deed and the payment of ad valorem stamp duty.1 Similarly, Gift Deeds for immovable property are compulsorily registrable regardless of the property's value.1 Under the Karnataka Stamp Act, gifts within family members (spouse, parents, children, siblings) are eligible for concessional fixed stamp duty rates, whereas non-family gifts are taxed as standard conveyances.3

### **2.2 Partition, Settlement, and Relinquishment Deeds**

* **Partition Deed:** Essential when joint owners or family members divide common property into separate shares. Registration establishes individual legal identities for each split portion.3  
* **Settlement Deed:** Used for the disposition of property during the lifetime of the owner, often for familial or charitable purposes. It requires registration to ensure the terms of settlement are legally binding.3  
* **Relinquishment Deed:** Executed when one co-owner gives up their share in a property in favor of another co-owner. This is frequently registered during the distribution of ancestral property following the death of a patriarch or matriarch.15

### **2.3 Power of Attorney and Developer Agreements**

* **Power of Attorney (PoA):** While a PoA for simple administrative tasks may be optional, a PoA coupled with consideration or authorizing the attorney to sell immovable property must be registered.12  
* **Developer-Buyer Agreements:** In the context of Bangalore's real estate market, Joint Development Agreements (JDA) between landowners and builders must be registered.16 Furthermore, Agreements to Sell that involve the transfer of possession (under Section 53A of the Transfer of Property Act) have been compulsorily registrable in Karnataka since 2001\.1

## **3\. Governing Bodies and Digital Data Sources**

Navigating the property registration landscape requires interaction with several interlinked government departments. The digitization of these departments under the Sakala Mission and the Kaveri 2.0 initiative has centralized many of their functions.4

### **3.1 Department of Stamps and Registration (IGR Karnataka)**

The Inspector General of Registration (IGR) oversees the hierarchy of registration offices. The local Sub-Registrar Office (SRO) is where the final physical verification and biometric signatures occur.1 The IGR maintains the Kaveri 2.0 portal, which serves as the "single window" for valuation, fee payment, and appointment booking.4

### **3.2 Bruhat Bengaluru Mahanagara Palike (BBMP) and BDA**

* **BBMP:** The municipal body for Bangalore, responsible for issuing the **Khata**, which records the property in the tax assessment register.20 As of October 2024, an **E-Khata** issued by BBMP is a mandatory prerequisite for any property registration in Bangalore.26  
* **Bangalore Development Authority (BDA):** For properties in BDA-developed layouts, the BDA issues allotment letters and possession certificates, which act as the foundational title documents for the first transfer.24

### **3.3 Revenue Department and the Bhoomi Ecosystem**

The Revenue Department manages agricultural land and its conversion for non-agricultural (NA) use. The **Bhoomi Portal** is the definitive source for the **RTC (Record of Rights, Tenancy, and Crops)** and **Mutation Extracts**.8 For rural or converted properties, the SRO verifies the Bhoomi database before allowing registration to prevent the sale of restricted or government lands.8

## **4\. Comprehensive Documentation Checklist**

A missing document can lead to the summary rejection of a registration application. The following checklist is exhaustive for properties in Bangalore.

### **4.1 Essential Property and Title Documents**

* **Original Sale Deed:** The instrument of transfer, typically prepared through the Kaveri 2.0 deed drafting module.5  
* **Mother Deed (Chain of Title):** Original documents tracing ownership for the preceding 30 years to ensure a clear title.13  
* **Encumbrance Certificate (EC):** An up-to-date EC (usually for 15-30 years) downloaded from Kaveri 2.0 to confirm the property is free of legal or financial liabilities.7  
* **Khata Certificate and Extract:** Specifically the digital **E-Khata** from BBMP, which proves the property's entry in municipal records.20  
* **Latest Property Tax Receipts:** Payment proof for the current financial year.20

### **4.2 Compliance and Institutional Documents**

* **RERA Registration:** For new apartments, the developer's RERA registration number and project completion certificate must be verified on the KRERA portal.31  
* **No Objection Certificate (NOC):** Required from banks if the property is mortgaged, or from the society/builder in certain resale scenarios.22  
* **Building Sanction Plan:** To verify that the construction is legal and as per approved norms.20  
* **Occupancy Certificate (OC) / Completion Certificate (CC):** Essential for flats to confirm the building is fit for habitation and complies with zoning laws.20

### **4.3 Identification and Personal Proofs**

* **Identity Proof:** Aadhaar Card and PAN Card for the buyer, seller, and two witnesses.16  
* **Photographs:** Recent passport-size photographs of all parties.22  
* **Power of Attorney (if applicable):** Registered PoA if the buyer or seller is not personally appearing.12

## **5\. Document Procurement and Appointment Scheduling**

The modernization of property services in Karnataka allows for most documentation to be procured through centralized online portals.

### **5.1 Kaveri Online Services Portal (Kaveri 2.0)**

The Kaveri portal ([kaveri.karnataka.gov.in](https://kaveri.karnataka.gov.in)) is the primary engine for property registration.

* **Registration:** Users must create a "Citizen Account" using their mobile number and Aadhaar-based OTP.4  
* **EC Procurement:** Select "Online EC," enter the property details (survey/site number or document number), pay the nominal fee online, and download the digitally signed PDF.4  
* **Valuation:** Use the "Property Valuation" tool to determine the government's guidance value, which is the higher of the market value or the consideration amount.4  
* **Pre-Registration Data Entry (PRDE):** This mandatory module requires uploading the draft sale deed, party details, and identity proofs. The system then generates a calculation of the total stamp duty and registration fees.5

### **5.2 BBMP e-Aasthi and Sevasindhu for E-Khata**

The procurement of an E-Khata is now integrated with the Sevasindhu portal.

* **Application:** Property owners log in to Sevasindhu, choose the "BBMP New E-Khata" service, and upload the registered deed, property photo, and BESCOM (electricity) ID.26  
* **Approval:** Once BBMP officials verify the SAS (Self Assessment Scheme) tax records, the digitally signed E-Khata is issued, which is then used in the Kaveri PRDE module.20

### **5.3 Booking the SRO Appointment**

Once PRDE is complete and fees are paid online through K2 (Khajane 2\) integrated payment gateway, the user can book a slot at the jurisdictional SRO.4

* **Selection:** Users can choose from available time slots (usually in 15-minute increments).7  
* **Confirmation:** An appointment slip is generated, which must be presented at the SRO along with original documents.5

## **6\. Fee Structure and Financial Calculations**

The cost of property registration in Karnataka is one of the highest in the region following significant upward revisions in 2025\.17

### **6.1 Stamp Duty Slabs (2025-26)**

Stamp duty is an ad valorem tax based on property value.16

* **Properties valued up to ₹20 Lakh:** 2%.16  
* **Properties valued ₹21 Lakh to ₹45 Lakh:** 3%.16  
* **Properties valued above ₹45 Lakh:** 5%.16

### **6.2 The 2025 Registration Fee Hike**

Effective **August 31, 2025**, the Government of Karnataka doubled the registration fee from 1% to **2%** of the property value.17 This change applies to all sale deeds, gift deeds, and conveyances registered after this date.39

### **6.3 Surcharges and Additional Cesses**

For properties within Bangalore's urban limits (BBMP/BDA), additional statutory charges apply:

* **Cess:** 10% on the stamp duty amount (earmarked for infrastructure development).16  
* **Urban Surcharge:** 2% on the stamp duty amount (applies to properties above ₹35 Lakh in urban zones).17  
* **Total Transaction Cost:** For a property above ₹45 Lakh, the effective cost totals approximately **7.6%** of the property value.38

### **6.4 Professional and Miscellaneous Fees**

* **Advocate/Document Writer Fees:** Professional drafting and title verification typically range from ₹15,000 to ₹50,000 in Bangalore.2  
* **Scanning and Indexing:** A fixed charge of ₹500–₹1,000 is collected at the SRO for digital archiving of the deed.16  
* **E-Stamp Commission:** For e-stamps above ₹5,000, no commission is charged; for lower amounts, a small fee of ₹10–₹15 applies.17

## **7\. Timeline from Preparation to Registered Deed**

The lifecycle of a property registration in Karnataka is structured around strict statutory windows and administrative processing times.

1. **Due Diligence and Document Collection (7–14 Days):** Procuring the Mother Deed, conducting a 30-year title search, and obtaining a fresh EC and E-Khata.10  
2. **Kaveri 2.0 PRDE and Payment (1–2 Days):** Entering data online, calculating fees, and making the digital payment.4  
3. **Appointment Waiting Time (2–5 Days):** Depending on the SRO's traffic, appointments are generally available within the week.21  
4. **Registration Day (1–3 Hours):** The physical visit to the SRO for biometric capturing and witness verification.4  
5. **Deed Delivery (Same Day to 2 Days):** While the scanned copy is often available on the Kaveri dashboard within hours, the physical original is usually returned to the buyer within 48 hours.4  
6. **Statutory Limit (4 Months):** Under Section 23 of the Registration Act, documents must be presented within four months of execution. A further four-month extension may be granted by the Registrar in cases of urgent necessity, subject to a penalty of up to 10 times the registration fee.1

## **8\. Critical Legal Provisions**

### **8.1 The Registration Act, 1908**

* **Section 17 (Compulsory):** Mandates registration for all non-testamentary instruments transferring property interests worth over ₹100.1  
* **Section 18 (Optional):** Allows optional registration for Wills and leases under one year.1  
* **Section 49 (Effect of Non-Registration):** The "punitive" section that strips unregistered documents of their evidentiary value in title disputes.1

### **8.2 The Karnataka Stamp Act, 1957**

This Act governs the taxation of instruments. Section 34 of this Act is particularly potent, stating that instruments not "duly stamped" (insufficiently paid duty) are inadmissible in evidence and may be impounded by the Sub-Registrar or a Court.46

### **8.3 The IT Act, 2000 and Digital Signatures**

Kaveri 2.0 relies on the legal validity of digital signatures established under Section 5 of the IT Act, 2000\.48 Digital Signature Certificates (DSC) and Aadhaar eSign are used for online document filing, though the final deed at the SRO currently requires biometric "live" signing to maintain high-security standards for immovable property transfers.4

### **8.4 RERA Act, 2016**

This federal law, implemented in Karnataka as the KRERA Rules, requires developers to deposit 70% of buyer funds into an escrow account and prohibits the sale of apartments in projects that do not have a valid RERA registration number.31

## **9\. Red Flags: Circumstances That Halt Registration**

Practitioners and buyers must remain vigilant for specific "stoppers" that can invalidate or delay the registration process.

* **Rajakaluve (Storm Water Drain) Encroachment:** Properties built on or near natural drains are a major risk in Bangalore. Under the Sept 2025 amendments, primary, secondary, and tertiary drains have strict buffer zones (up to 75m) where no construction is permissible.34  
* **B-Khata Complications:** A B-Khata is not a title document; it is a record of tax payment for unauthorized properties. While the High Court has at times eased registration for B-Khata properties, they generally cannot obtain occupancy certificates or formal bank loans.20  
* **Measurement Mismatch:** If the dimensions in the Sale Deed do not match the physical boundaries or the survey sketch, the registration will be blocked until a **Rectification Deed** is executed by the previous seller.45  
* **Pending Litigation (Lis Pendens):** Any active court stay on a survey number will be reflected in the Bhoomi or Kaveri systems, leading to an immediate halt in registration.13  
* **Guidance Value Undervaluation:** If the transaction price is lower than the government-mandated guidance value for that specific ward/street, the system will force the calculation of duty based on the higher guidance value.16

## **10\. Consequences of Skipping or Improper Registration**

The risks of failing to register a property transaction correctly are categorical and often irreparable.

* **Lack of Ownership Proof:** In India, the Sale Deed is the only document that legally transfers title. Without registration, the seller remains the legal owner, regardless of any money paid or possession taken.1  
* **Loan Rejection:** No bank in Karnataka will provide a mortgage or home loan without a registered Sale Deed and an "unbroken" EC for at least 13-15 years.5  
* **Inability to Mutate (Khata Transfer):** BBMP and the Revenue Department will not update the owner's name in their records without a registered deed.8 This prevents the buyer from paying property tax, obtaining electricity (BESCOM) connections, or getting building plan approvals.20  
* **Loss of Investment to Third Parties:** If the seller fraudulently registers the same property to a second buyer who acts in good faith and registers their deed first, the second buyer's claim will generally supersede the first unregistered buyer's claim.1  
* **Eviction Risk:** In cases of illegal layouts or encroachment on government land (e.g., Rajakaluve), the absence of proper registration and sanctioned plans can lead to summary demolition by BBMP or BDA without compensation.34

| Risk Category | Consequence of Improper Registration | Primary Legal/Admin Impact |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Title** | Title remains with the previous owner. | Loss of absolute ownership rights.1 |
| **Financial** | Ineligibility for home loans or refinancing. | Rejection by all scheduled banks.20 |
| **Evidentiary** | Deed inadmissible in a court of law. | Cannot defend property in court.1 |
| **Taxation** | Cannot pay property tax in the buyer's name. | Accumulation of arrears in the old owner's name.20 |

## **Conclusion**

The property registration system in Karnataka is a high-stakes legal environment where procedural precision is as important as the underlying contract. The evolution of the Kaveri 2.0 portal has significantly reduced the scope for manual error, but it has simultaneously increased the "hard-block" barriers for non-compliant properties, particularly those missing E-Khatas or those encroaching on Rajakaluve buffer zones.4 For legal knowledge bases and property practitioners, the critical takeaway for 2026 is that the doubling of registration fees and the tightening of digital verification loops make pre-registration due diligence mandatory rather than elective. A registered deed is the essential certificate of the transaction, but its strength is entirely dependent on the integrity of the documentation chain and the municipal compliance of the property it describes.12
