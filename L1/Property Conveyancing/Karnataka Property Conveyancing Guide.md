# **Professional Reference Manual: Property Conveyancing and Legal Transfers in Karnataka**

Property conveyancing in the state of Karnataka, and specifically within the rapidly expanding urban landscape of Bangalore, has evolved into a sophisticated legal discipline requiring rigorous adherence to statutory protocols, digital governance platforms, and evolving fiscal mandates. This reference document provides an exhaustive technical analysis of the conveyancing lifecycle, designed for legal practitioners, researchers, and institutional stakeholders within the Karnataka jurisdiction.

## **1\. Legal Definition and Structural Components of Conveyancing**

Conveyancing is the technical legal process through which the title to immovable property is transferred from a transferor to a transferee. In the Karnataka context, this process is not merely a documentation exercise but a multi-tiered legal audit that ensures the "marketability" of a title.1 It transitions property rights from a contractual state—often initiated by an *Agreement to Sell*—to a vested legal state through the execution and registration of a *Sale Deed*.1

The structural components of conveyancing are defined by three distinct phases:

* **Title Investigation**: This involves a retrospective forensic audit of the property's history. In Karnataka, this investigation must typically span a minimum of 30 to 50 years to establish an unbroken "Chain of Title," ensuring that the current seller possesses a valid and absolute interest to convey.3  
* **Deed Drafting**: This is the creative legal phase where the rights, liabilities, and specific covenants of the parties are articulated. Under Section 54 of the Transfer of Property Act, a sale is the transfer of ownership for a price paid or promised.5 Drafting must account for the undivided share (UDS) in land for apartments, easement rights, and warranties against encumbrances.1  
* **Registration**: Pursuant to the Registration Act, 1908, and state-specific amendments, registration is the mandatory act of recording the transaction in public books. This serves as "constructive notice" to the world, precluding third-party claims based on ignorance of the transaction.7

The legal importance of this process in Karnataka cannot be overstated; an unregistered sale deed for property valued above ₹100 is inadmissible in evidence and fails to create any legal interest in the property.8

## **2\. Trigger Events: When Conveyancing is Mandated**

The requirement for formal conveyancing arises across a diverse spectrum of real estate transactions, each governed by specific legal nuances and documentation requirements.

### **2.1 Freehold Property and Allotments**

Freehold transfers are the most common trigger, involving the total transfer of ownership. In Bangalore, this frequently includes properties allotted by the Bangalore Development Authority (BDA) or the Karnataka Housing Board (KHB). Such conveyances require the verification of the original Allotment Letter, Possession Certificate, and the subsequent absolute Sale Deed.10

### **2.2 Leasehold and Assignment Transfers**

Leasehold transfers do not convey absolute ownership but rather the right to possess and use the property for a specified term. These are common in industrial areas (KIADB) or older BDA layouts. The conveyancing instrument here is typically an *Assignment Deed* or a *Transfer of Lease*, where the remaining term of the lease is conveyed to the new party.1

### **2.3 Developer-Buyer Transactions**

This involves under-construction or newly completed apartments and gated communities. The process is bifurcated: initially, an *Agreement to Sell* is executed (often registered under RERA norms), followed by a *Sale Deed* once the developer obtains the Occupancy Certificate (OC). The *Joint Development Agreement (JDA)* between the landowner and the developer serves as the "root of title" in these scenarios.1

### **2.4 Gift, Inheritance, and Family Settlements**

Conveyancing is mandated even in non-commercial transfers. A *Gift Deed* is used for voluntary transfers without consideration, while a *Release Deed* or *Partition Deed* is used among legal heirs to consolidate or divide ancestral property.1 Karnataka offers specific stamp duty concessions for "family members," a category strictly defined by state law.12

### **2.5 NRI and Cross-Border Transactions**

Non-Resident Indians (NRIs) and Overseas Citizens of India (OCIs) are governed by the Foreign Exchange Management Act (FEMA). While they can acquire residential and commercial properties, they are prohibited from purchasing agricultural land or farmhouses unless through inheritance.14 Conveyancing for NRIs involves strict compliance with RBI remittance rules, often requiring payments through NRE/NRO accounts and specialized TDS certificates.16

## **3\. Statutory Oversight: Governing Bodies and Data Sources**

The conveyancing process in Karnataka is integrated with multiple governmental data sources that provide a transparent, albeit complex, verification environment.

* **Inspector General of Registration (IGR) / Kaveri 2.0**: This is the central repository for all registered property transactions in Karnataka. The *Kaveri 2.0* portal reveals ownership history through Encumbrance Certificates (EC) and provides certified copies of previously registered deeds.19  
* **Bruhat Bengaluru Mahanagara Palike (BBMP)**: The BBMP maintains the "Khata" system, which is essentially the property tax register. It reveals the tax assessment status and municipal recognition of the property. The *E-Aasthi* platform is the modern digital interface for these records.10  
* **Bangalore Development Authority (BDA)**: For properties in BDA-developed layouts, this body provides allotment records, layout plans, and the critical "Possession Certificate." It also verifies whether the property is subject to any acquisition for public infrastructure.3  
* **Real Estate Regulatory Authority (RERA) Karnataka**: For any project exceeding 8 apartments or 500 sq. meters, RERA provides a digital audit trail of building approvals, promoter financial stability (via escrow accounts), and progress reports.3  
* **Revenue Department (Bhoomi Portal)**: Crucial for properties that were once agricultural. It reveals the *Record of Rights, Tenancy and Crops (RTC)*, mutation history, and whether the land is "prohibited" under the PTCL Act or other social welfare legislations.7  
* **Judiciary (District and High Courts)**: While not a traditional "data source" for registration, a litigation search in court records is essential to reveal pending partition suits, stay orders, or insolvency proceedings against the seller.9

## **4\. Comprehensive Technical Checklist for Conveyancing**

A professional conveyancing audit must systematically verify the following documentation to ensure a legally sound transfer.

### **4.1 Root of Title and Chain Documents**

* **Mother Deed (Parent Deed)**: The document tracing ownership back 30–50 years. Any gap in this chain is a fatal title defect.3  
* **Registered Sale Deed**: The immediate preceding title document in the name of the current seller, duly stamped and registered.1

### **4.2 Encumbrance and Liability Records**

* **Encumbrance Certificate (EC)**: Form 15 reflects all registered transactions (sales, mortgages). Form 16 (Nil EC) is issued if no transactions are recorded. A 30-year EC is the gold standard for Bangalore transactions.4  
* **Property Tax Receipts**: Verification of tax paid for the current and previous three financial years, ensuring no arrears that could lead to a charge on the property.3

### **4.3 Municipal and Planning Compliance**

* **Khata Certificate and Extract**: Proof of entry in municipal records. "A-Khata" denotes compliance with building bylaws, while "B-Khata" is a temporary acknowledgment of a property that lacks certain approvals or is on unauthorized revenue land.21  
* **Occupancy Certificate (OC)**: Issued by the local authority (BBMP/BDA) confirming the building is constructed as per the sanctioned plan and is safe for habitation.4  
* **Completion Certificate (CC)**: Confirms the construction of the building is complete in accordance with the standards.4  
* **Sanctioned Building Plan**: Must be cross-verified against the actual structure to detect "deviations".3

### **4.4 Regulatory and Zonal Clearances**

* **DC Conversion Order**: Essential for properties on converted agricultural land, proving that the change of land use (CLU) was approved by the Deputy Commissioner.3  
* **RERA Registration**: Verification of the project's registration on the Karnataka RERA portal to ensure compliance with timelines and disclosure norms.4  
* **No Objection Certificates (NOCs)**: From the Fire Department, BWSSB, and BESCOM.4

## **5\. Procedural Roadmap: Obtaining Critical Documentation**

The acquisition of documents in Karnataka has been streamlined through various portals, though certain legacy records still require physical intervention.

### **5.1 The Kaveri 2.0 Ecosystem**

* **Online EC Application**: Users register on the Kaveri 2.0 portal, enter property details (Survey Number, Village, Ward), and pay the search fee (approximately ₹35 for the first year and ₹10 for subsequent years). The digitally signed EC is typically available for download within 24–48 hours.20  
* **Certified Copies (CC)**: To obtain a copy of a registered Sale Deed, users apply via the "Certified Copy" service on Kaveri 2.0. This requires the registration number and year of the document.29

### **5.2 The Sevasindhu and BBMP Portals**

* **E-Khata Registration**: As of late 2024, E-Khata is mandatory for property registrations. Owners apply through the *Sevasindhu Karnataka* portal or the *BBMP E-Aasthi* platform. Required documents include the Sale Deed, EC, Latest Tax Receipt, and Aadhaar.10  
* **Khata Transfer (Mutation)**: Following a sale, the buyer must apply for a name change (mutation) on the BBMP portal. This involves uploading the newly registered Sale Deed and paying a fee of approximately 2% of the property value.21

### **5.3 Offline Registries and Revenue Offices**

* **Sub-Registrar Offices (SRO)**: While data entry is done online via Kaveri 2.0, the physical presence of the buyer, seller, and two witnesses is mandatory at the SRO for biometric verification and final signing.7  
* **Assistant Revenue Officer (ARO)**: For legacy properties or resolving Khata discrepancies, a physical visit to the jurisdictional BBMP ward office is often necessary.3

## **7\. Conveyancing Lifecycle and Timelines**

The end-to-end conveyancing process in Bangalore typically spans 30 to 60 days, depending on the complexity of the title and the responsiveness of governmental portals.

* **Phase 1: Due Diligence (7–15 Days)**: Involves the collection of documents from the seller and the 30-year EC search. This phase is often the most time-consuming if manual records from the 1980s or 90s must be retrieved.3  
* **Phase 2: Legal Opinion and Drafting (3–5 Days)**: The advocate reviews the documents and drafts the *Agreement to Sell* or the *Sale Deed*. If a bank loan is involved, the bank’s legal panel will conduct a parallel review.3  
* **Phase 3: Pre-Registration Data Entry (1–2 Days)**: Uploading details to Kaveri 2.0, paying the stamp duty, and securing a "Slot Booking" for registration.3  
* **Phase 4: Registration Day (Same Day)**: The physical process at the SRO takes 1–3 hours, involving biometric verification and signing.3  
* **Phase 5: Post-Registration (15–45 Days)**: Includes the physical delivery of the original Sale Deed and the subsequent application for Khata transfer and mutation.3

## **8\. Governing Legal Provisions and Statutory Mandates**

Conveyancing in Karnataka is anchored in several federal and state statutes that define the validity and consequences of property transfers.

### **8.1 The Transfer of Property Act (TPA), 1882**

* **Section 54**: Defines a sale and mandates that any transfer of tangible immovable property of value over ₹100 must be by a registered instrument. It distinguishes between a "Sale" and a "Contract for Sale," the latter creating no interest in the property itself.2  
* **Section 55**: Outlines the statutory duties of the seller and buyer. The seller is bound to disclose material defects in the property or title, while the buyer has a duty to disclose facts that materially increase the property's value.18  
* **Section 58**: Governs the creation of mortgages. A conveyancer must check for "Equitable Mortgages" (created by deposit of title deeds), which may not always appear in the EC.5

### **8.2 The Registration Act, 1908 (Karnataka Amendments)**

This act mandates the registration of any instrument that creates, declares, or assigns an interest in immovable property. Karnataka has added Section 32A, making the affixing of photographs and fingerprints of the executants compulsory.39

### **8.3 The Prohibition of Benami Property Transactions Act**

This act prohibits transactions where the consideration is paid by one person and the property is held in the name of another (unless for certain family exceptions). Benami properties are subject to confiscation by the Central Government without compensation.41

### **8.4 The RERA Act, 2016 (Karnataka Rules)**

Section 13 of RERA prohibits builders from receiving more than 10% of the cost as an advance without entering into a registered *Agreement for Sale*. RERA also mandates that the developer rectify any structural defects discovered within five years of possession.3

## **9\. Red Flags and Critical Risks in the Bangalore Market**

The Bangalore real estate market is susceptible to certain jurisdictional-specific risks that can invalidate a conveyancing process.

* **B-Khata and Revenue Sites**: Bangalore is saturated with "Revenue Sites"—land that has not been legally converted (DC Conversion) for residential use. While BBMP issues "B-Khata" (a tax register entry), these properties are technically illegal, cannot be mortgaged to nationalized banks, and are prone to demolition.28  
* **Missing Occupancy Certificate (OC)**: Purchasing an apartment without an OC is a significant risk. It indicates that the builder has likely deviated from the sanctioned plan. Owners of such flats may face utility disconnections and inability to secure an A-Khata.4  
* **PTCL Act Violations**: Under the *Karnataka Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes (Prohibition of Transfer of Certain Lands) Act*, any sale of land originally granted to SC/ST individuals without government permission is void *ab initio*. Such land can be reclaimed by the government at any time.43  
* **Unresolved Partition and Minor’s Interest**: Ancestral property sold without the consent of all legal heirs or without court permission for a minor’s share is a recipe for future title disputes.1  
* **GPA and JDA Risks**: Transactions based on a *General Power of Attorney (GPA)* from a landlord to a developer require extreme scrutiny. If the GPA is revoked or if the landlord dies before the sale deed is registered, the developer loses the legal authority to transfer the property.3

## **10\. Legal and Financial Consequences of Defective Conveyancing**

Deficient conveyancing results in more than just administrative delays; it can lead to total financial loss and perpetual litigation.

* **Defective Title and Litigation**: A cloud on title allows third-party claimants (missing heirs or prior mortgagees) to file for a *Declaration of Title* or *Partition*. In Bangalore, title disputes in civil courts can last between 5 and 15 years.46  
* **Mortgage Rejection**: Nationalized and major private banks will reject home loan applications for properties that lack an A-Khata, OC, or a clear 30-year chain. This drastically reduces the resale potential and market value of the asset.4  
* **Confiscation under Benami Act**: Properties found to be "Benami" are confiscated by the state. Recent Supreme Court rulings (e.g., *S. Rajendran v. DCIT*) clarify that Benami attachments cannot even be stayed by insolvency tribunals, reinforcing the absolute risk of such transactions.48  
* **Demolition and Illegal Structure Liability**: Under the *Akrama Sakrama* logic and municipal laws, structures with more than 15–50% deviation are ineligible for regularization. Owners may be forced to demolish illegal portions or face continuous municipal penalties.50  
* **RERA Penalties for Developers**: Homebuyers who have undergone proper conveyancing can leverage RERA for interest on delayed possession. In *CMP/210310/07776*, a Bangalore homebuyer won ₹29 lakh in interest due to the builder's failure to hand over possession with an OC on time.52

## **11\. Conclusion: The Future of Conveyancing in Karnataka**

The transition to a fully digital conveyancing landscape in Karnataka—anchored by Kaveri 2.0 and the E-Khata mandate—marks a paradigm shift towards transparency and fraud prevention. However, this digitalization does not negate the need for traditional legal due diligence. The recent fiscal revisions (August 2025\) and the 7.6% statutory cost mandate a more strategic approach to property budgeting. For professional peers and institutional investors, the " Bangalore Model" of conveyancing now demands a hybrid expertise: a deep understanding of historical revenue laws combined with proficiency in modern digital governance portals. Ensuring a clear, marketable title remains the single most effective safeguard against the systemic risks of the Karnataka real estate market.
