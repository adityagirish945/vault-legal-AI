**1\. LEGAL DEFINITION: PROPERTY TAX NAME CHANGE, KHATA TRANSFER, AND THEIR RELATIONSHIP IN BBMP**  
**1.1 What Is "Property Tax Name Change"?**  
"Property Tax Name Change" refers to the administrative process by which the name of the owner recorded on BBMP property tax records \- including the tax demand notice, property tax receipt, and the municipal property register \- is updated to reflect the current legal owner of the property. The term is used colloquially by citizens and legal practitioners to describe any change to the name associated with a property in BBMP's fiscal records.  
Formally, this is not a standalone administrative process in BBMP's system. Instead, it is the downstream consequence of completing a Khata Transfer or Khata Mutation \- that is, once the Khata (the official BBMP property register entry) is updated to the new owner's name, the property tax records automatically reflect the same name.  
**1.2 What Is a Khata?**  
A Khata (also spelled "Khatha") is the official municipal record maintained by BBMP that establishes a person's legal standing as the owner/occupier of a specific property for the purpose of property tax assessment and payment. The Khata contains:

* Owner's name and contact details  
* Property Identification Number (PID) \- now the electronic e-PID, a unique 10-digit number  
* Property location, ward number, and address  
* Dimensions, built-up area, and land area of the property  
* Property tax assessment details (Unit Area Value, zone, usage type)  
* GPS coordinates (for e-Khata entries)

There are two physical types of Khata issued by BBMP:

* A-Khata: Issued to properties compliant with BBMP building regulations, zoning laws, and having all requisite approvals. Eligible for home loans, building plan approvals, and all legal transactions.  
* B-Khata: Issued to properties with compliance deficiencies (unapproved constructions, title irregularities, BDA/layout violations). Has limited legal recognition; not eligible for most bank loans or building permits. B-Khata can be converted to A-Khata on payment of Betterment Charges (typically 0.5%-1.5% of land market value) once compliance is achieved.

**1.3 What Is Khata Transfer / Khata Mutation?**  
Khata Transfer (also called Khata Mutation) is the formal BBMP process of transferring the Khata entry \- and consequently the property tax liability \- from the previous owner's name to the new owner's name following any change in property ownership. The terms "Khata Transfer" and "Khata Mutation" are used interchangeably in BBMP's official communications and in Karnataka property practice.  
Specifically:

* Khata Transfer: Denotes the process when an existing Khata is re-registered in the transferee's name after a change of ownership (sale, gift, inheritance, court order).  
* Khata Registration: Refers to creating an entirely new Khata for a property that has never been registered with BBMP (newly constructed/developed properties, newly converted layouts).  
* Khata Mutation: The general municipal law term for the administrative update of property ownership records; used interchangeably with Khata Transfer for existing properties.

**1.4 Are "Property Tax Name Change" and "Khata Transfer" the Same or Different in BBMP?**  
They are substantively the same process, described from different perspectives, but differ in nuance:

| Dimension | Explanation |
| :---- | :---- |
| Formal Process Name | In BBMP's official system, the process is called Khata Transfer (offline) or e-Khata Mutation (online via e-Aasthi portal). There is no separate standalone process called "Property Tax Name Change." |
| Relationship | Completing a Khata Transfer automatically updates the property tax records. The property tax name change is the result, not a separate step. |
| BBMP Form Used | BBMP Form 4 (Application for Khata Transfer / Name Change). Submitted to the Assistant Revenue Officer (ARO) of the ward. |
| Why the Confusion? | Citizens receive property tax bills in the previous owner's name and seek a "name change" on the tax receipt. BBMP processes this as a Khata mutation, after which future tax receipts bear the new owner's name. |
| Practical Implication | Applicants must always file for Khata Transfer/Mutation; once approved, the tax name changes automatically. Attempting to change only the tax name without a Khata update will be rejected by BBMP. |
| e-Khata (2025 System) | Under the current e-Aasthi digital system, mutation (Khata Transfer) and property tax name update happen in the same integrated workflow. The Aadhaar-based automatic mutation launched in August 2025 further integrates this. |

**⚠ NOTE:** Important Legal Distinction: Mutation/Khata Transfer does NOT confer title to property. Legal title passes upon registration of the sale deed/gift deed/probate at the Sub-Registrar's office under the Registration Act, 1908\. Mutation updates municipal administrative records for tax purposes only. Mutation is not a substitute for title investigation.  
**2\. WHEN IS PROPERTY TAX NAME CHANGE MANDATORY \- ALL TRIGGERING SCENARIOS**  
A Khata Transfer and consequent Property Tax Name Change is mandatory (and must be applied for within a reasonable time of ownership change) in all the following scenarios:  
**2.1 Purchase of Property (Sale)**  
After the registration of a sale deed at the Sub-Registrar's office, the buyer must apply for Khata Transfer. The property tax liability passes to the new owner from the date of registration. All future tax notices and payment obligations attach to the new owner. Failure to complete Khata Transfer leaves the seller's name in BBMP records, creating liability ambiguity and practical obstacles for the buyer.  
**2.2 Inheritance \- With a Will (Testate Succession)**  
When a property is bequeathed by a will (testament), the beneficiary must obtain a Probate of the Will (in cases where required under the Succession Act) or a succession certificate, and then apply for Khata Transfer in their name. In practice, BBMP accepts a registered Will along with the death certificate and legal heir affidavit for mutation purposes in many cases, subject to ARO discretion.  
**2.3 Inheritance \- Without a Will (Intestate Succession)**  
Where the property owner dies without a will, the legal heirs succeed to the property by operation of law (Hindu Succession Act, 1956; Indian Succession Act, 1925; Muslim Personal Law, etc.). The heirs must jointly apply for mutation, submitting a legal heir certificate/succession certificate and a death certificate. If heirs wish to mutate in one person's name only, a Relinquishment Deed (Hakkuthyaga) or Partition Deed must be registered and submitted to BBMP.  
**2.4 Gift Deed**  
After registration of a Gift Deed under the Registration Act, 1908, the donee (recipient) must apply for Khata Transfer. The gift deed must be duly stamped (stamp duty applicable in Karnataka on gift deeds) and registered. Unregistered gifts are not recognised by BBMP for Khata mutation purposes.  
**2.5 Court Order / Decree**  
Where a civil court, family court, or other competent tribunal passes a decree transferring ownership (e.g., partition suit, decree for specific performance, matrimonial property settlement), the decree-holder must submit a certified copy of the court order to BBMP for mutation.  
**2.6 Partition Deed**  
Where co-owners partition a jointly-held property by way of a registered Partition Deed, Khata must be bifurcated and individual Khata entries created for each new owner's share. This requires a Khata Bifurcation application in addition to, or instead of, a standard Khata Transfer.  
**2.7 Government Allotment / Auction**  
Properties acquired through government allotment (e.g., BDA sites, BBMP leases, Karnataka Housing Board allotments) or government auction require Khata registration/transfer in the allottee's name based on the allotment letter or sale certificate issued by the authority.  
**2.8 Company / Corporate Ownership Changes**  
Where a company owns property and undergoes a name change, merger, demerger, or change of registered office, BBMP requires an application for Khata amendment supported by relevant corporate documents (Certificate of Incorporation, MCA filings, board resolutions).

**4\. STEP-BY-STEP OFFICIAL PROCESS FOR EACH SCENARIO**  
**4A. After Property Purchase (Sale Deed)**  
**Track A: Aadhaar-Based Automatic Mutation (Recommended \- Launched August 2025\)**  
This is the fastest and most recommended route for straightforward post-sale mutations where both seller and buyer have Aadhaar seeded in the e-Khata system.

* Visit the official e-Aasthi portal: [https://bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov.in/](https://bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov.in/)  
* Select the option: "eKhata Automatic Mutation" from the home page.  
* Enter the property's e-PID (Electronic Property Identification Number), obtainable from the registered sale deed, existing Khata certificate, or latest property tax receipt.  
* Complete Aadhaar e-KYC for both the Seller (transferor/previous owner) and the Buyer (transferee/new owner). Authentication is via Aadhaar-linked OTP or biometric.  
* Upload mandatory documents (see Section 5A for complete checklist). Scanned PDF/JPEG copies are accepted.  
* Pay the applicable processing fee online (₹200-₹500, depending on property category).  
* A 7-day public objection window opens after submission. If no objections are lodged, the system auto-approves the mutation.  
* Upon auto-approval, the e-Khata is updated in the buyer's name. The property tax records and utility connections (BESCOM/BWSSB) automatically reflect the change.  
* Download the updated e-Khata certificate (digitally signed PDF) from the portal.

**⚠ NOTE:** This Aadhaar-based automatic mutation route is available only where (a) the property already has an active e-Khata with Aadhaar seeding, (b) the e-Khata details match the seller's identity exactly, and (c) there are no disputes on the property. Mismatches in names, undivided shares, or properties with pending litigation must be processed through Track B.  
**Track B: Standard Online Application via e-Aasthi (Non-Aadhaar / Complex Cases)**

* Visit [https://bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov.in/](https://bbmpeaasthi.karnataka.gov.in/) and create/log in to your citizen account (mobile OTP-based).  
* Search for your property using e-PID, SAS application number, or property address details.  
* Select "Apply for Khata Transfer / Mutation."  
* Fill in the online application form with new owner details, property details, and transaction type (Sale).  
* Upload all required documents (see Section 5A).  
* Submit the application and pay the fee online. An acknowledgement with a Sakala application number is generated.  
* BBMP ARO reviews the application. A field inspection may be conducted.  
* If documents are complete and no objections arise, the ARO recommends approval; the RO approves and the e-Khata is updated.  
* Timeline: 15-30 working days in standard cases.  
* Download the updated e-Khata once approved.

**Track C: Offline Application at BBMP Office (Legacy / Offline Cases)**

* Obtain BBMP Form 4 (Khata Transfer Application Form) from the BBMP ward office, relevant ARO office, Bangalore One Centre, or download from the BBMP website.  
* Fill in Form 4 with all property and new owner details.  
* Compile the complete document set (see Section 5A).  
* Submit the application with original and photocopy document sets to the ARO of the ward where the property is located.  
* Obtain an acknowledgement receipt with an application/Sakala number for tracking.  
* Pay Khata transfer fee (2% of stamp duty paid at registration, minimum ₹500) at any Bangalore One Centre using the Sakala application number.  
* BBMP officials verify documents and may conduct a physical site inspection.  
* The Khata Transfer is sanctioned, typically within 2-3 working days after fee payment once documents are verified. Overall timeline: 30-90 working days depending on complexity.  
* Collect the updated Khata Certificate and Khata Extract from the ARO office or download via the e-Aasthi portal.

**4B. After Death / Inheritance (Intestate and Testate)**  
**Intestate Succession (No Will)**

* Obtain a legal heir certificate from the Tahsildar (revenue officer) or a succession certificate from the civil court, as applicable.  
* Obtain the death certificate of the deceased owner (issued by BBMP/Municipal Authority).  
* If all legal heirs agree to transfer to one heir's name, execute and register a Relinquishment Deed (Hakkuthyaga) or Partition Deed before the Sub-Registrar.  
* Prepare a family tree/genealogy affidavit on Rs. 200 non-judicial stamp paper, attested by a Notary.  
* File an indemnity bond on Rs. 200 stamp paper, signed by all legal heirs, indemnifying BBMP against future claims.  
* Apply for Khata Transfer via the e-Aasthi portal (Track B above) or offline (Track C above), uploading/submitting the inheritance-specific documents along with standard documents.  
* BBMP issues a public notice (at the ARO office and/or ward office board) for a specified period (typically 15 days) inviting objections.  
* If no objections are received, ARO approves the mutation.  
* Updated Khata is issued in the heir's name.

**Testate Succession (With a Will)**

* If probate is required (for Christians and Parsis under the Indian Succession Act, 1925, and in cases of contested wills), obtain a grant of Probate from a competent civil court.  
* For Hindu/Muslim testators and uncontested wills, BBMP may accept a registered Will along with the death certificate and legal heir affidavit without requiring probate, subject to the ARO's discretion.  
* Submit the Will (original or certified copy), death certificate, probate order (if obtained), and supporting affidavits to BBMP.  
* Proceed with the standard application process (Track B or Track C above).  
* Follow the public notice and objection period before final approval.

**4C. After Gift Deed**

* Ensure the Gift Deed has been duly executed, stamped (stamp duty in Karnataka on gift deed: 5% of market value for blood relatives, standard rates for others), and registered at the Sub-Registrar's office.  
* Obtain the registered Gift Deed (certified copy with Sub-Registrar's endorsement).  
* Obtain an Encumbrance Certificate (Form 15 / Form 16\) from the Sub-Registrar's office for the period from the date of gift to the present, confirming no encumbrances or other transactions.  
* Apply for Khata Transfer via the e-Aasthi portal or offline at the ARO office.  
* For the Aadhaar automatic mutation route (Track A), both donor and donee must authenticate via Aadhaar e-KYC.  
* Pay the applicable mutation/transfer fee.  
* Upon approval (standard 7-day auto-approval under Aadhaar track, or 15-30 working days under standard track), the Khata and property tax records update in the donee's name.

**8\. RELEVANT LEGAL PROVISIONS**  
**8.1 Primary Legislation**

* Bruhat Bengaluru Mahanagara Palike Act, 2020 (Karnataka Act 53 of 2020): The principal legislation governing BBMP's constitution, powers, functions, and revenue collection. Replaced the earlier Karnataka Municipal Corporations Act, 1976 (KMC Act) for BBMP specifically. Under the BBMP Act, 2020, Chapter XII governs property taxes, assessment, and maintenance of property records.  
* Karnataka Municipal Corporations Act, 1976 (KMC Act): The parent statute for municipal corporations in Karnataka (still applicable to other municipal corporations, and many of its principles and sections are carried forward/mirrored in BBMP Act 2020). Key provisions relevant to Khata/mutation: Section 114 (Preparation and maintenance of property records by municipal authorities); Section 120 (Assessment and collection of property taxes based on Khata entries).  
* BBMP (Property Tax Assessment, Recovery and Management) Rules, 2024: Delegated legislation under the BBMP Act, 2020\. Contains detailed rules on self-assessment, property tax computation (Unit Area Value system, six value zones), payment, and recovery.  
* Karnataka Land Revenue Act, 1964: Governs mutation of rural land records (RTC). Sections 128, 129, 133, and 136 deal with Records of Rights and mutations. These principles are adopted by analogy in BBMP's urban Khata mutation process.  
* Registration Act, 1908 (Central Act): Governs mandatory registration of property transfer documents (sale deeds, gift deeds, partition deeds, etc.). Registration is a prerequisite for Khata mutation \- unregistered instruments are not accepted by BBMP.  
* Transfer of Property Act, 1882 (Central Act): Governs the law of sale, mortgage, gift, and other property transfers. Relevant for understanding when title passes (upon registration) versus when mutation occurs.  
* Indian Succession Act, 1925 (Central Act): Governs testamentary succession (Wills) and intestate succession for Christians and Parsis. Relevant for probate requirements.  
* Hindu Succession Act, 1956: Governs intestate succession for Hindus, Buddhists, Jains, and Sikhs. Relevant for determining legal heirs in inheritance mutation cases.  
* Karnataka Stamp Act, 1957: Governs stamp duties on instruments executed in Karnataka. Relevant for stamp duty on sale deeds and gift deeds, which forms the basis for the 2% Khata transfer fee calculation.  
* Karnataka Right to Services Act, 2011 (Sakala Act): Mandates time-bound delivery of designated government services, including Khata Transfer. Provides statutory right to appeal against delay or refusal.  
* Right to Information Act, 2005 (Central Act): Enables citizens to seek information about application status and reasons for delay or rejection from BBMP.

**8.2 Government Orders and Circulars**

* e-Aasthi Project Launch Order: BBMP introduced the e-Aasthi (e-Khata) system through a government order directing digitisation of all property records in Bangalore. By January 2025, BBMP declared all properties within its jurisdiction to have been digitised.  
* Aadhaar-Based Automatic Mutation Circular (August 2025): BBMP issued a circular/order launching the Aadhaar-linked automatic e-Khata mutation service via the e-Aasthi portal, providing for auto-approval after a 7-day objection window when both parties authenticate via Aadhaar e-KYC.  
* BBMP Betterment Charges Rules: Govern the charges applicable for regularisation/conversion of B-Khata properties, relevant where a Khata transfer also triggers a compliance issue.  
* Sakala Service Charter (Khata Services): Lists the SLA for Khata Transfer under the Right to Services Act.

**9\. COMMON REJECTION REASONS**  
BBMP may reject or hold a Khata mutation application for the following reasons:

* Incomplete Documentation: Missing any mandatory document from the checklist (e.g., no Encumbrance Certificate, no previous Khata certificate, missing ID proof). This is the most frequent reason for rejection or delay.  
* Pending Property Tax Dues: Any outstanding property tax amount \- including penalties and interest \- must be fully cleared before the mutation application will be processed. BBMP verifies tax payment status before approval.  
* Name Mismatch: Discrepancies between the name on the sale deed, the name in the existing Khata, and the name on the Aadhaar/ID proof of either party. Even minor spelling variations (e.g., 'K Sanjay' vs 'Sanjay K') can trigger rejection. Requires a sworn affidavit or marriage certificate to explain variation.  
* Property Details Mismatch: Discrepancy in property dimensions, survey number, or PID between documents and BBMP records. Requires correction application before mutation can proceed.  
* B-Khata / Unapproved Property Issue: Properties with B-Khata status arising from unapproved construction or layout violations may face additional scrutiny. Mutation may be blocked pending regularisation under an applicable scheme.  
* Unregistered Instrument: Submitting an unregistered sale agreement, unregistered gift deed, or unregistered Will (unless accompanied by probate) as the basis for mutation \- BBMP requires registered instruments.  
* Legal Dispute / Litigation: If there is an active litigation, court injunction, or any encumbrance (mortgage, attachment, lis pendens) on the property, BBMP will not process the mutation until the dispute is resolved or the injunction is vacated.  
* Aadhaar Not Seeded (for Automatic Mutation): The previous owner's Aadhaar number not being linked to the e-Khata record prevents the Aadhaar automatic mutation route.  
* Kaveri Data Mismatch: For online applications, if the document deed number entered does not match Kaveri Online Services records (use the EC number, not the deed registration number in some cases), the system may return an error blocking the application.  
* Objections from Third Parties: Any co-owner, neighbouring property owner, legal heir, or other interested party filing an objection during the public notice period will put the mutation on hold pending ARO inquiry.  
* Missing Apartment Association NOC: For properties in apartment complexes, the absence of a No Objection Certificate from the registered Owners' Association is a common rejection ground.  
* Incomplete Heir Documentation (Inheritance): In inheritance cases, failure to obtain a legal heir certificate covering all heirs, or absence of a relinquishment deed where one heir seeks sole mutation, will result in rejection.  
* Property in Encroachment / Government Land: If BBMP's records or satellite data indicate the property may overlap with government land (roads, drains, parks, reservations), the application is flagged for "Verification for Government Land" and the ARO/RO cannot proceed without field verification.  
* e-Aasthi System / Technical Issues: Portal connectivity errors, document upload failures, or data migration gaps from older manual Khata records can cause technical rejection. Escalation via helpline (9480685700) or visit to ARO office is needed.

**10\. CONSEQUENCES OF PROPERTY TAX RECORDS REMAINING IN PREVIOUS OWNER'S NAME**  
Failure to update BBMP property tax records (i.e., failure to complete Khata Transfer and mutation) after a change of ownership has wide-ranging legal, financial, and transactional consequences for the new owner:  
**10.1 Legal Consequences**

* Weak Evidentiary Position: Municipal records (Khata) in the previous owner's name create an evidentiary gap in the new owner's chain of title. While Khata is not a title document per se, its absence in the new owner's name weakens their standing in any property dispute.  
* Risk of Adverse Claims: If the previous owner's heirs or creditors assert claims over the property, the new owner's delayed mutation makes it harder to rebut the claim promptly.  
* Property Cannot Be Re-registered: In a future sale, the buyer and their banker will insist on an updated Khata in the seller's name. Sub-Registrars increasingly require a current e-Khata for property registration.  
* Building Plan and Occupancy Certificate Issues: BBMP will not process building plan approvals, modification permits, or issue occupancy certificates for a property whose Khata is not in the current owner's name.  
* Contempt / Tribunal Consequences (Statutory Non-Compliance): Although Karnataka law does not prescribe a criminal penalty for failure to mutate, persistent non-compliance with tax obligations (flowing from un-mutated property) can lead to BBMP initiating recovery proceedings.  
* Title Complications in Inheritance: If the property passes through multiple generations without mutation at each stage, reconstructing the chain for a eventual sale becomes complex, expensive (requiring extensive documentary evidence and possibly a court order), and can delay a transaction indefinitely.

**10.2 Financial Consequences**

* Property Tax Liability Ambiguity: Tax bills continue to be raised in the previous owner's name. The new owner may not receive tax notices, leading to accumulation of arrears, penalties (2% per month), and potential recovery action \- even though the new owner is legally liable.  
* Accumulation of Penalties: If tax goes unpaid because the new owner does not receive bills, BBMP can impose penalties of 2% per month (24% per annum). The new owner inherits this liability.  
* Inability to Avail 5% Early Payment Rebate: The new owner cannot pay tax under their own name and thus cannot avail the 5% rebate for early payment (by July 31 of the assessment year), leading to higher net tax outgo.  
* Home Loan / Mortgage Issues: Banks and housing finance companies require updated Khata in the borrower's name as a mandatory document for home loans, Loan Against Property (LAP), and property-backed overdrafts. Un-mutated property cannot be mortgaged or pledged as security.  
* Attachment / Recovery Risk: BBMP can attach and auction a property for persistent non-payment of property tax. If the Khata remains in the previous owner's name and tax is not paid by either party, the new owner faces the risk of BBMP action against the property.

**10.3 Transactional / Commercial Consequences**

* Inability to Resell: Prospective buyers will conduct due diligence and will refuse to purchase a property whose Khata / property tax records are not in the seller's name. The property becomes practically unsaleable until mutation is completed.  
* Inability to Obtain Utility Connections (New): BESCOM (electricity) and BWSSB (water) require an updated Khata / e-Khata in the applicant's name for new connections, change of name on existing connections, or enhancement of load. An un-mutated property cannot get new utility connections in the new owner's name.  
* Inability to Apply for Building Permits: BBMP requires Khata in the applicant's name for sanctioning new construction, additions, alterations, or demolition of any structure on the property.  
* Delayed / Blocked NRI Transactions: NRI owners residing abroad who have not mutated their inherited property face particular difficulties \- they cannot complete future transactions without updating records, and the process requires physical presence or a valid Power of Attorney.  
* Insurance and Property Valuation Issues: Insurance companies and valuers may flag un-mutated property as a risk factor, affecting insurability and property valuations for financial transactions.  
* RERA / Developer Disputes: Where the property is part of a RERA-registered project and the builder has not obtained individual Khata for flat owners, the flat owner lacks a complete municipal ownership record \- this is a matter of legal dispute and affects the flat owner's ability to independently transact.

**10.4 Remedies If Mutation Was Long Delayed**  
Where mutation was not completed at the time of acquisition and a significant period has elapsed, the following steps should be taken:

* Compile the full chain of ownership documents from the last mutated owner to the present owner, including all intermediate registered deeds.  
* Clear all accumulated property tax arrears (with penalties) for the period of ownership.  
* File a comprehensive Khata Transfer application supported by an affidavit explaining the delay, all title documents, and payment receipts.  
* Where records have been lost or the property was never registered in BBMP's system, apply for new Khata registration via the e-Aasthi portal.  
* If BBMP refuses mutation due to complexity, seek legal advice on filing a Writ Petition before the Karnataka High Court compelling BBMP to process the mutation, or approach the Karnataka Lokayukta for grievance redressal.
