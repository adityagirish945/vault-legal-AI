# **Comprehensive Legal Treatise on the Framework and Jurisprudence of Agreements to Sell in Karnataka**

The conveyance of immovable property in India, particularly within the specific regulatory ecosystem of Karnataka, is governed by a sophisticated matrix of central and state legislations. At the heart of this process lies the Agreement to Sell (ATS), an executory contract that serves as the blueprint for the eventual transfer of title. While the Sale Deed constitutes the final act of transfer, the ATS establishes the contractual obligations, fiscal liabilities, and legal safeguards that define the relationship between a vendor and a vendee. This report provides an exhaustive analysis of the legal, fiscal, and procedural dimensions of Agreements to Sell under Indian property law, with a dedicated focus on the unique requirements and judicial trends prevalent in Karnataka.

## **The Jurisprudential Foundation: Legal Distinctions and Ownership Rights**

The distinction between an Agreement to Sell and a Sale Deed is the most critical conceptual boundary in property law. Under Section 54 of the Transfer of Property Act (TPA), 1882, a "sale" is defined as a transfer of ownership in exchange for a price paid or promised.1 A Sale Deed is an executed contract that results in the immediate transfer of all rights, titles, and interests from the transferor to the transferee.1 In contrast, an Agreement to Sell is an executory contract—a promise that a transfer shall take place on settled terms at a future date.1

The fundamental legal difference resides in the nature of the rights created. A Sale Deed creates a right *in rem*, which is a right against the world at large, effectively establishing the buyer as the absolute owner upon registration.1 An Agreement to Sell, however, creates only a right *in personam*, a personal right against the seller to compel the execution of a Sale Deed.1 Crucially, Section 54 explicitly clarifies that a contract for sale does not, by itself, create any interest in or charge on the subject property.4 This principle ensures that ownership remains vested in the seller until the final conveyance is registered, meaning the seller retains the right to possess and enjoy the property, as well as the risk of its destruction, unless otherwise agreed.1

The transition of an Agreement to Sell into a specifically enforceable instrument occurs when the conditions precedent are satisfied and the parties demonstrate readiness and willingness to perform their respective obligations. While an ATS does not pass title, it provides the legal basis for the buyer to seek a decree of specific performance under the Specific Relief Act, 1963, compelling the seller to finalize the transaction.2

## **The Statutory Superstructure: Governing Laws and Judicial Shifts**

The legal life of an Agreement to Sell is sustained by several key statutes. The Indian Contract Act, 1872, dictates the essential validity of the agreement, requiring competent parties, free consent, and lawful consideration.2 Section 10 of the Contract Act serves as the gateway; if the parties are minors, of unsound mind, or disqualified by law, the agreement is *void ab initio*.5

### **The 2018 Paradigm Shift in Specific Performance**

The Specific Relief Act, 1963, underwent a transformative amendment in 2018, which fundamentally altered the landscape of property litigation. Prior to this amendment, specific performance was a discretionary remedy.6 Courts typically viewed monetary compensation (damages) as the primary relief for a breach of contract, granting the actual performance of the sale only in exceptional circumstances where damages were deemed inadequate.6

Post-2018, specific performance has been elevated to a substantive right.10 Section 10 now mandates that specific performance *shall* be enforced by the court, subject only to specific exceptions listed in Sections 11(2), 14, and 16\.10 This shift provides immense protection to buyers in Karnataka's volatile real estate market, as it prevents sellers from unilaterally backing out of deals to pursue higher offers.13 Furthermore, Section 20 of the amended Act introduces "substituted performance," allowing a buyer to complete the contract through a third party at the defaulting seller's expense.11 The adjudication of these suits is now subject to a twelve-month timeline, aimed at reducing the "decade-long court battles" traditionally associated with property disputes in Bengaluru.13

### **The Registration and Stamp Act Interface**

In Karnataka, the fiscal dimensions are governed by the Karnataka Stamp Act, 1957, and the Registration Act, 1908\. While an Agreement to Sell is generally not compulsorily registrable to prove a contract, its admissibility in evidence for claiming "part performance" under Section 53A of the TPA is strictly contingent upon registration.7 The Karnataka government has recently intensified these requirements through the Registration (Karnataka Amendment) Bill, 2025, which seeks to integrate property registration with electronic revenue databases (Kaveri and Bhoomi) to curb the menace of unauthorized layouts and fraudulent transactions.17

## **Mandatory Structure and Essential Clauses of a Professional Agreement**

A robust Agreement to Sell must be drafted with linguistic precision to avoid the "ambiguity" that often triggers litigation.9 A typical professional agreement in Karnataka follows a structured progression:

### **1\. Title, Date, and Parties**

The document must clearly identify the Vendor and the Vendee. Accuracy in these details is paramount, especially regarding legal heirs, as their inclusion ensures that the agreement remains binding even upon the death of a party.3 In Karnataka, it is standard practice to include the father's name, age, and PAN details for identity verification.3

### **2\. Recitals of Title and Flow of History**

The "Recitals" section serves as the historical narrative of the property. It must detail the "chain of title" for at least the preceding 30 years, explaining how the current seller acquired the property—whether through a Sale Deed, Gift Deed, Inheritance, or Court Decree.4 In Bengaluru, where ancestral properties are common, verifying the "Partition Deed" or "Succession Certificate" is a critical requirement for a clear marketable title.8

### **3\. Comprehensive Property Description**

The "Schedule Property" description is the technical anchor of the ATS. It must specify the survey number, hissa number, village, taluk, and precise boundaries.3 In Karnataka's revenue system, it is well-settled that boundaries prevail over the stated extent of the land in case of discrepancy, making the accurate marking of North, South, East, and West mandatory for enforceability.24

### **4\. Consideration Structure and Payment Schedule**

This clause defines the total sale price and the breakdown of payments. It typically includes:

* **Earnest Money (Advance):** A deposit made to signal the buyer's commitment.25  
* **Balance Payment:** Milestones tied to specific conditions, such as the clearing of a loan or the issuance of a Khata certificate.4  
* **Mode of Payment:** Specificity here (Cheque/RTGS) ensures a clear bank record and reduces the risk of cash-related disputes.20

### **5\. Time-is-of-Essence and Timeline**

In property transactions, time is not automatically the essence unless explicitly stated.29 A "Time-is-of-Essence" clause establishes a hard deadline for the execution of the Sale Deed, providing the non-defaulting party with a clear right to rescind the contract if the other party fails to meet the timeline.4

## **Earnest Money Dynamics and TDS Obligations**

Earnest money serves as the "good faith deposit" that binds the parties together.25 In Karnataka, standard practice typically dictates an earnest money deposit ranging from 2% to 10% of the property's total value, though this can escalate in highly competitive markets like Silicon Valley-Bengaluru or for new constructions.26

### **Forfeiture vs Refund Conditions**

The conditions governing this deposit must be explicit. Generally, if the buyer defaults on the payment schedule or "gets cold feet" without valid legal grounds, the seller is entitled to forfeit the earnest money as compensation for taking the property off the market.4 Conversely, if the seller fails to provide a clear title or defaults on obligations, the buyer is entitled to a full refund, often with an equivalent amount as a penalty.4

### **Section 194IA: The TDS Mandate**

A critical fiscal obligation for the buyer is the deduction of Tax Deducted at Source (TDS) under Section 194IA of the Income Tax Act. For any property transaction (excluding agricultural land) valued at ₹50 lakhs or more, the buyer must deduct 1% TDS from the sale consideration.36

| Feature of Section 194IA | Requirement |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Applicability Threshold** | Total purchase value ![][image1] ₹50 Lakhs 36 |
| **TDS Rate** | 1% of total sale consideration (20% if no PAN provided) 36 |
| **Timing of Deduction** | At the time of *each* payment, including advances/installments 36 |
| **Deposit Mechanism** | Online filing of Form 26QB using the buyer's PAN 36 |
| **Seller's Certificate** | Buyer must issue Form 16B to the seller within 15 days of deposit 37 |
| **Inclusions** | Car parking, club membership, maintenance fees are all taxable 36 |

Failure to comply with these TDS rules can lead to severe penalties, including interest charges of 1% to 1.5% per month and fines for late filing.41

## **Condition Precedents and Revenue Verification**

The "road map" of a property transaction is often defined by condition precedents—events that must occur before the final Sale Deed can be registered.31 In Karnataka, these are heavily focused on revenue and municipal compliance.

1. **Mutation and Khata Transfer:** Ownership in civic records (Khata) must match the registered Sale Deed. For resale properties, the buyer often makes the "Khata Transfer" in the seller's name a prerequisite for final payment to ensure future loan eligibility and resaleability.27  
2. **Clearing Loans and NOCs:** If the property has an existing mortgage, the seller must produce a "No Objection Certificate" (NOC) and a Discharge Certificate from the bank.23  
3. **Revenue Documents Audit:** A meticulous verification of the **RTC (Record of Rights, Tenancy, and Crops)** or "Pahani" is mandatory for converted or agricultural lands. The RTC shows ownership, cultivation history, and any existing liabilities or mortgages.47  
4. **Building Plan and Occupancy Certificate (OC):** For apartments and constructed villas, the buyer must verify the sanctioned building plan and OC issued by authorities like the BBMP or BDA.23

## **Protective Clauses for the Buyer: Securing the Investment**

Given that real estate transactions involve significant life savings, the buyer must incorporate "safety net" clauses.14

* **Right to Inspect Title:** The agreement must provide the buyer with the right to inspect all original title deeds and revenue records prior to the final payment.4  
* **Lien Rights under Section 55(6)(b):** Unless the buyer has improperly declined delivery, they possess a "statutory charge" or lien on the property for the purchase money and interest paid. This charge is enforceable even against subsequent buyers who have notice of the payment.51  
* **Indemnity against Third-Party Claims:** This is a "non-negotiable" clause that secures the buyer against future disputes arising from prior legal heirs' claims, undisclosed mortgages, or tax arrears.4  
* **Caveat Filing:** To prevent the seller from alienating the property to a third party during the ATS period, buyers often file a "caveat" in the civil court to be notified of any pending litigation involving the property.23

## **Protective Clauses for the Seller: Ensuring Performance and Liquidity**

Sellers require protection against non-performing buyers or market fluctuations.13

* **Forfeiture of Earnest Money:** As noted, this protects the seller from financial loss and time wasted if the buyer backs out without cause.4  
* **Interest on Delayed Payments:** To deter delays, sellers should include a penalty interest rate (standardly 12% to 18% per annum) for any payments not made according to the schedule.28  
* **Right to Rescind and Termination by Notice:** A clear clause allowing the seller to rescind the contract after a specific notice period (e.g., 30 days) if the buyer remains in breach. This allows the seller to "unlock" the property and sell it elsewhere.20  
* **Time Limitation:** Specifying that the agreement will expire automatically after a certain period if conditions are not met prevents the property from being "locked" in an indefinitely valid agreement.15

## **Stamp Duty and Registration in Karnataka: The Article 5(e) Framework**

Fiscal compliance in Karnataka is governed by the Karnataka Stamp Act, 1957\. The categorization of the Agreement to Sell under the "Articles" determines the duty payable.

### **Mandatory Registration post-2017**

While the central Registration Act might suggest optional registration for an ATS, Karnataka has streamlined its process to ensure higher compliance. The implementation of the **RERA** (Real Estate Regulatory Authority) in 2017 and the shift toward **e-Khatas** in September 2024 has made registration of agreements practically essential for any subsequent legal action or municipal update.19

### **Stamp Duty Rates under Article 5(e)**

Article 5(e) governs agreements relating to the sale of immovable property.

| Scenario | Stamp Duty Rate | Registration Fee |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **ATS without Possession (Article 5(e)(ii))** | 0.5% of the total consideration (cap removed in 2024\) 58 | ₹20 (nominal) 60 |
| **ATS with Possession (Article 20 \- Conveyance)** | 5% (treated as a deemed Sale Deed) 60 | 1% of value (2% after 31 Aug 2025\) 50 |
| **Conveyance (Final Sale Deed)** | 2% to 5% based on value slabs 50 | 2% of value (after 31 Aug 2025\) 50 |

**Important Distinction:** If an agreement contains a recital that possession is delivered or "agreed to be delivered" before the conveyance, it attracts the full conveyance duty of 5%. This is a common pitfall where parties unknowingly trigger a 5% liability on an interim agreement.61

## **Section 53A TPA: The Doctrine of Part-Performance**

Section 53A provides an equitable defense to a buyer who has fulfilled their contractual part but lacks a registered title deed. It allows the buyer to act as a "shield" against a seller trying to reclaim possession.16

### **Mandatory Registration for Section 53A**

Since the **Registration (Amendment) Act, 2001**, a document containing a contract for transfer for consideration must be registered to invoke Section 53A protection.7 An unregistered agreement is inadmissible in evidence for the purpose of proving part performance and cannot confer any power under Section 53A.7 Karnataka High Court rulings, such as in *Giriyappa v. Kamalamma*, emphasize that the exception must be strictly construed and that possession obtained without a registered instrument is not protected under this doctrine.16

## **Common Disputes: Top 10 Litigation Scenarios in Karnataka**

Property litigation in Karnataka often arises from "loophole" drafting.9 Below are the top scenarios identified in the region:

1. **Ancestor/Minor Property Claims:** Heirs of the seller claim the property was ancestral and sold without their consent.35 *Solution:* Include all adult heirs as witnesses/confirming parties and specify legal necessity.  
2. **Unilateral Termination by Seller:** Seller cancels the ATS due to rising market prices.31 *Solution:* Explicit termination clauses requiring court intervention or heavy penalties.  
3. **Ambiguity in Possession:** Conflict over whether "symbolic" or "physical" possession was handed over.62 *Solution:* Precise definitions of the "Possession Date" and "Vacant Possession" requirement.  
4. **Financial Distress vs. Sale Intent:** Seller claims the agreement was a loan in disguise.56 *Solution:* Detailed recitals confirming the sale price matches the market/guidance value.  
5. **Boundary and Encroachment Errors:** Neighbors push walls beyond survey lines after the ATS is signed.15 *Solution:* Pre-registration survey and sketch (Tippani/RTC) verification.  
6. **"B-Khata" Regularization Issues:** Buyer discovers the property cannot be regularized for an "A-Khata" after paying the advance.27 *Solution:* Clear warranty that the property is "A-Khata" compliant and free of deviations.  
7. **Delayed Possession by Builders:** Builders fail to deliver projects on time, leading to RERA/Consumer Forum suits.23 *Solution:* Strict timelines with specific per-month late fees.  
8. **Default in TDS Payment:** Revenue department holds the seller liable for the buyer's failure to deduct TDS.37 *Solution:* Prerequisite for final registration is the submission of Form 26QB challan.  
9. **Fraudulent GPA Transactions:** General Power of Attorney holder sells the property after the principal has died or revoked the GPA.23 *Solution:* Requirement for a fresh "Life Certificate" of the principal at the time of registration.  
10. **Revenue Record Discrepancies:** Conflict between the Sale Deed description and the online Bhoomi/Kaveri records.47 *Solution:* Mandatory electronic integration verification before registration as per 2025 amendments.

## **Expert Sample Clauses for Professional Sale Agreements**

The following clauses represent the standard for high-value real estate transactions in Karnataka:

### **1\. Consideration and Payment Schedule**

"The Total Sale Consideration for the Schedule Property is fixed at ₹ \[Amount\] (Rupees Only). The Vendee has this day paid a sum of ₹ \[Amount\] (Rupees Only) as Earnest Money Deposit via Cheque No. \[Number\] dated drawn on, the receipt of which is hereby acknowledged by the Vendor. The balance consideration of ₹ \[Amount\] shall be paid by the Vendee at the time of registration of the Sale Deed before the jurisdictional Sub-Registrar. It is explicitly agreed that the Vendee shall deduct TDS at 1% of the total consideration as per Section 194IA and deposit the same with the Income Tax Department, providing the Vendor with Form 16B within 15 days of such deposit." 3

### **2\. Default by Buyer and Forfeiture**

"If the Vendee fails to fulfill the payment obligations within the stipulated period, the Vendor shall be entitled to forfeit the entire Earnest Money Deposit and rescind this Agreement. The Vendor shall provide the Vendee with a 30-day notice to cure such default before exercising the right of forfeiture. Upon rescission, all rights and interests of the Vendee in the Schedule Property shall cease immediately." 4

### **3\. Default by Seller and Damages**

"If the Vendor fails to provide a clear and marketable title or refuses to execute the Sale Deed despite the Vendee being ready and willing to perform, the Vendor shall refund the entire advance amount along with an equivalent amount as penalty (Totaling 200% of the Earnest Money). The Vendee shall also have the right to seek specific performance of this contract under the Specific Relief Act, 1963, and recover all associated legal expenses from the Vendor." 30

### **4\. Time is of the Essence**

"Both parties hereby agree that the time for completion of the sale transaction, fixed at \[Number\] days from the date of this Agreement, is the essence of the contract. Any extension of time must be mutually agreed upon in writing and signed as an addendum to this Agreement." 29

### **5\. Possession and Delivery**

"The Vendor shall deliver vacant, peaceful, and physical possession of the Schedule Property to the Vendee at the time of registration of the Sale Deed. The Vendor shall ensure that all tenants, if any, are vacated and all utility arrears (electricity, water, and property tax) are cleared up to the date of possession." 3

### **6\. Warranty of Title and Indemnity**

"The Vendor hereby warrants that they are the absolute and lawful owner of the Schedule Property and have a valid and marketable title free from all encumbrances, liens, mortgages, litigations, or governmental acquisitions. The Vendor further agrees to indemnify and hold the Vendee harmless against any loss, damage, or legal costs arising from any future challenge to the title or any undisclosed liability pertaining to the period prior to the date of Sale Deed." 3

## **Final Jurisprudential Synthesis**

In the contemporary legal landscape of Karnataka, the Agreement to Sell has evolved from a simple preliminary contract into a highly regulated fiscal and proprietary instrument. The 2018 shift in the Specific Relief Act has effectively neutralized the "seller's whim," making the performance of the contract a general rule rather than an exception. However, this increased protection for the buyer is balanced by rigorous fiscal requirements under the Income Tax Act and the Karnataka Stamp Act. For professional peers in the legal and real estate sectors, the successful execution of these agreements depends on an exhaustive audit of revenue records (RTC/Khata), a precise articulation of payment milestones, and the strict adherence to registration mandates to ensure that the document serves as a "shield" in the courtroom and a "sword" in the market.
